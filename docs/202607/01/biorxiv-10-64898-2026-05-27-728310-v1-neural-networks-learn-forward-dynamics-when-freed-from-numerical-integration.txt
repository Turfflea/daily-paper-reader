## ABSTRACT 16 

Seamless interaction between humans and machines requires interfaces that remain robust to the 17 variability inherent in biological signals and physical environments. Advanced human-machine 18 interfaces (HMIs) increasingly rely on machine learning to predict or control limb dynamics. These 19 systems must learn input-to-output mappings between control variables and limb state, such as the 20 mapping from muscle forces or joint torques acting about segmented arm joints to limb posture 21 over time. Such statistical input-to-output transformations can result in numerical instability of 22 predicted musculoskeletal kinematics and dynamics. Achieving the robustness of biological motor 23 control requires solving both forward and inverse dynamics problems; however, these problems 24 are computationally asymmetric because they entail opposing operations–integration and 25 differentiation. Since we have previously shown that neural networks solve the inverse dynamics 26 problem when trained to map kinematic to dynamic signals during reaching, we hypothesized that 27 representing separately the approximation of equations of motion (EOM) and their temporal 28 numerical integration may capture the relevant computational structure of the forward dynamics 29 problem. We tested this hypothesis by comparing a conventional direct-mapping recurrent neural 30 network (RNN) with a two-stage model, the artificial physics engine (APE). When predicting the 31 state of a two-segment system under external perturbations not encountered during training, the 32 direct-mapping, monolithic model produced large prediction errors inconsistent with the expected 33 interaction torque, whereas the APE maintained low error and remained stable under novel initial 34 conditions and perturbations. Mapping system dynamics in the terms of the EOM improves 35 robustness against intrinsic and extrinsic sources of variability by imposing a causal, physics-based 36 structure on HMI design. 37

Simulating dynamics with neural networks 149 

After training, the networks were used in their respective models (APE or direct mapping) to predict 150 joint angles across tests in unperturbed and perturbed movement conditions. Movements spanned 151 all ordered start-end pairs among 11 targets not seen during training. For the APE, the prediction 152 for a given movement was initialized with its initial kinematic state (the first sample of the reference 153 data). At each subsequent step, the embedded network used the current torques and the kinematic 154 state from the previous iteration step to predict the instantaneous joint accelerations. Joint velocities 155 and angles were then updated using explicit Euler integration ( 𝛥𝑡 = 0.1 ms) of the predicted 156 accelerations. This ‘predict–integrate’ cycle, in which the newly computed state becomes the input 157 to the next step, was repeated until the end. Importantly, except for the first state-initialization step, 158 reference kinematic data were not used in this process. State estimation relied solely on 159 numerically integrating the network-predicted accelerations, driven by the torque stream. For the 160 direct RNN, the instantaneous torques were the only inputs used to predict joint kinematics. 161 Predictions were generated independently for each point over the entire movement without explicit 162 numerical integration. 163

## RESULTS 172 

In this study, we show that simulating forward dynamics with neural networks requires decoupling 173 state-dependent dynamics from numerical integration. Monolithic, direct-mapping models conflate 174 these computational steps, increasing representational complexity and failing to approximate 175 perturbed movements. By contrast, the decoupled architecture of our APE model isolates the 176 equations of motion and delegates state integration to an external solver. This modular approach 177 reduces prediction error compared with the monolithic baseline. APE generalizes to novel initial 178 conditions, unseen perturbations, while exhibiting emergent intersegmental dynamics, confirming 179 that the model internalizes the system’s physics rather than memorizing trajectories. 180

Under matched training protocols, the direct model, with end-to-end mapping from joint torques to 182 joint angles, yielded substantially higher prediction errors than the modular APE model. As 183 illustrated by examples of both unperturbed and perturbed movements (Fig. 1c), the direct model 184 performed poorly at generalizing across trials and failed to associate input torques with the 185 corresponding movement evolution. Decoupling physics from integration 205 

The APE model was configured to treat the mapping as an initial value problem and yielded 206 significantly more accurate and robust predictions than the direct mapping approach. Figure 1c 207 shows representative examples where APE predictions (red) closely tracked reference trajectories 208 during both unperturbed and perturbed trials. The APE achieved mean RMSE values of 1.38 ± 209 0.83° for the shoulder and 2.50 ± 0.86° for the elbow in unperturbed trials, representing an 11-fold 210 and 5-fold reduction in error compared to the direct mapping approach (Fig. 2a; p < 0.01, post-hoc 211 pairwise comparison with Dunn-Sidak correction). The model maintained high accuracy when 212 external perturbations were applied, with error values of 1.39 ± 0.84° for the shoulder and 2.50 ± 213 0.87° for the elbow that were not significantly different from unperturbed conditions (Fig. 2b; p > 214 0.05, post-hoc pairwise comparison with Dunn-Sidak correction). This stability, maintained even 215 when faced with new initial conditions and substantial external perturbations (no external 216 perturbations were seen during training), suggests that the model had internalized the system’s 217 EOM. 218

## DISCUSSION 238 

In this study, we demonstrate that decoupling the process of integration from the equations of 239 motion, rather than simulating a direct trajectory mapping, provides a more computationally 240

We hypothesized that the predictive instability 241 of monolithic neural networks in forward dynamic simulations stems from conflating two distinct 242 computational steps: (1) approximating physical laws and (2) performing numerical integration. 243 When these processes are entangled, structural inaccuracies are recursively embedded during 244 implicit integration, driving a ‘vicious cycle’ of error accumulation. To resolve this, we developed an 245 artificial physics engine (APE) that explicitly decouples these processes. Using a shallow RNN to 246 approximate the instantaneous accelerations of a limb model—and delegating numerical 247 integration to a dedicated external solver—the APE achieved a five- to tenfold improvement in 248 predictive accuracy over traditional monolithic networks. This physics-embedded architecture not 249 only generalized robustly to unencountered initial conditions and perturbations but also 250 successfully reproduced complex, interaction-torque-driven intersegmental dynamics, confirming 251 the true embedding of underlying mechanical laws. Ultimately, these findings support the broader 252 

principle that the computational tractability of learning movement dynamics depends not only on 253 

network capacity, but also on the representation in which the problem is posed. 254 The primary limitation of our study is the reliance on a simple two-segment mechanical model to 255 represent the upper arm and forearm movement within a vertical plane. While this model does not 256 provide sufficient detail to simulate fine motor tasks, such as hand-object manipulation, finger 257 coordination, and detailed contact mechanics, it is sufficient to probe whether a learning system 258 captures coupled multi-joint dynamics. Two-segment models remain valuable because they 259 incorporate multi-segment coupling; their dynamics include inertial, Coriolis, and centripetal 260 interaction terms absent in single-joint movements. This level of modeling is often sufficient to 261 determine whether a method captures the essential properties of a coupled articulated structure 262 and is commonly used to represent human biomechanics during planar reaching, describing how 263 torque terms, including intersegmental interaction terms, shape movement (Gritsenko et al., 2009; 264 Hollerbach and Flash, 1982). Similar mechanical complexity is also relevant to gross locomotor 265 modeling that captures coordination and energetics (Darici and Kuo, 2022; Faraji and Ijspeert, 266 2017; Lin et al., 2020; Saunders et al., 1953; Taga et al., 1991), with applications in prosthetics. 267

Biomimetic learning of system dynamics via prediction error 270 

The APE’s acquisition of the system dynamics of the double pendulum mimicked the hypothesized 271 acquisition of bodily dynamics by the internal forward model within the CNS. Such a process is 272 driven by sensory prediction errors, computed by comparing expected and actual outcomes. In the 273 biological motor system, the forward internal model processes the efference copy of the motor 274 command and generates a prediction of the expected sensory consequences, such as the 275 dynamics of the effector (Wolpert and Flanagan, 2001); our artificial engine used an analogous 276 process: the RNN processed the torque command and generated a prediction of the pendulum’s 277 dynamics, with the actual dynamics reported by the physics engine serving as the ‘sensory’ 278 feedback. The discrepancy between predicted and sensory feedback constituted the sensory 279 prediction errors. These errors are widely believed to drive synaptic modification within the 280 biological circuits forming the forward internal model (Shadmehr et al., 2010). Analogously, in the 281 neural network within the APE, prediction errors were used to tune the weight matrices via a 282 learning rule. This error-driven tuning enabled the APE to accurately respond to unseen external 283 perturbations, functionally replicating the entrained adaptive responses well-documented in 284 biological motor control studies, for example, force-field learning paradigms (Shadmehr and 285 Mussa-Ivaldi, 1994; Thoroughman and Shadmehr, 2000). 286 

Overcoming learning and simulation challenges 287 

A primary obstacle to designing accurate and stable noninvasive HMIs is the non-stationary 288 relationship between EMG signals and movement (see Scheme and Englehart, 2011 for review), 289 as these signals are continuously modulated by intrinsic and extrinsic factors in real-time. For 290 conventional data-driven models that learn this direct mapping, non-stationarity poses a critical 291 problem known in deep learning as covariate shift , where the statistical distribution of the input data 292

This shift results in low, hard-to-learn performance that may degrade as the interface 294 parameters change, leading to the need for frequent recalibrations. The evidence of out-of-sample 295 prediction of interaction torques with APE indicates that the network simulates a causal, physical 296 relationship between applied forces and resulting motion. 297 However, the current APE implementation, which was focused on high physical precision, has not 298 achieved real-time performance (using a standard personal computer) with the implementation 299 limited to the choice of a fixed-step explicit Euler integrator to propagate the system’s state. To 300 ensure numerical stability and accuracy when simulating the complex, nonlinear, and sometimes 301 stiff dynamics of the two-segment arm, a small, fixed time step ( 𝛥𝑡 = 0.1 ms) was necessary, but 302 incompatible with the strict time budgets of interactive applications like prosthetic control or virtual 303 reality. The typical goal for a full simulation and rendering cycle should be less than 33 ms to 304 maintain the illusion of smooth motion (Dinev et al., 2018), with the physical simulation loop latency 305 under 16 ms (Erez et al., 2015), and even shorter latencies for the accurate computations of muscle 306 models. An important future direction for this work is, therefore, to transition the APE from a fixed-307 step to an adaptive step-size integration scheme. Such predictive allocation of computational 308 resources is computationally biomimetic, since biological motor control is inherently dynamic, 309 allocating neural resources to meet task demands (Todorov, 2004). 310 An alternative to the numerical solver is an architecturally suitable neural network, such as a 311 residual neural network. Characterized by skip connections of the form ℎ)0- = ℎ) + 𝑓 (ℎ )),312 where ℎ) is the hidden state at layer 𝑙 (representing a discrete step of numerical integration) 313 and 𝑓 is a learned transformation modeling system dynamics, a residual network is mathematically 314 equivalent to the forward Euler discretization of an underlying ODE (also see Chen et al., 2019; He 315 et al., 2015). Hence, the APE could, in principle, be formulated as a two-stage neural architecture: 316 (1) a recurrent network to approximate the EOM, and (2) a residual network trained to perform the 317 temporal integration.
