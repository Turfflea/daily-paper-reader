Abstract: 23 Accurate design of hydrogen-bonding (H-bonding) interactions is a longstanding goal in 24 protein design, as they can facilitate specific protein-protein interactions while improving 25 the solubility of the proteins in the unbound state. Despite this, computational design of H-26 bond networks remains underexplored in the deep learning era. Here, we present 27 HBDesigner, a novel algorithm for H-bond network design. Through a combination of deep 28 learning-based sampling and atomistic energy scoring, HBDesigner outperforms existing 29 tools in designing connected H-bond networks onto protein sca_olds. We demonstrate the 30 usefulness of HBDesigner by creating monomeric proteins with buried polar interactions 31 and homodimers with extended interface H-bond networks, and by installing specificity 32 into a family of homologous heterodimers where prior design tools fail to do so. The ability 33 to design H-bond networks into arbitrary protein sca_olds should be broadly useful for a 34 wide range of design applications. 35

Introduction 36 

Hydrogen bonds (H-bonds) play a critical role in dictating protein structure and function. 37 Enzymes use H-bonds to preorganize key groups for catalysis, enhancing e_iciency by 38 reducing the entropic cost of binding 1. Binding a_inity 2 and specificity 3 of protein-protein 39 interactions can also be driven by H-bonding contacts, which require precise geometric 40 compatibility between subunits 4. For minibinders and protein-based nanoparticles, 41 increasing interface polarity can enhance solubility and reduce aggregation 5.42 Despite these factors, tools for de novo protein design have historically struggled to design 43 protein pockets and interfaces with H-bonding networks comparable in complexity to those 44 observed in naturally occurring proteins 4,6 . This is a challenging design problem because H-45 bonds are only energetically favorable within a small range of distances and angles 7, and 46 because buried unsatisfied heavy polar atoms (BUHs) are highly unfavorable 8. Even in the 47 era of deep learning (DL) based protein design, most de novo binder design campaigns still 48 rely on hydrophobic contacts to achieve strong experimental success rates 4,9,10 . This strategy 49 can limit the scope of viable targets, while many therapeutically relevant target classes such 50 as antigen-antibody complexes exhibit polar interfaces 11 .51 Previous e_orts to design buried H-bond networks led to the Rosetta HBNet protocol 12 ,52 which searches for combinations of sidechain rotamers capable of H-bonding when 53 installed onto a given backbone. This approach has been successfully applied to several de 54 novo systems, including pH-sensitive assemblies 13 and transmembrane b-barrels 14 . A later 55 

update replacing exhaustive network enumeration with Monte Carlo sampling (MC HBNet) 56 improved its performance on large, asymmetric systems 8, facilitating the design of 57 orthogonal heteromeric interfaces 15 . While e_ective, MC HBNet su_ers from a few 58 limitations, including a reliance on fixed rotamer libraries and di_iculty modeling long, 59 flexible amino acids. More recently, DL design tools such as ProteinMPNN 16 and PIPPack 17 

60 have demonstrated significant advancements in sequence design and sidechain packing, 61 respectively, in comparison to empirical methods such as Rosetta. ProteinMPNN in 62 particular has been used to improve the polarity of designed protein-protein interfaces 5.63 However, these and other DL design tools lack the atomistic energy function necessary to 64 explicitly model H-bond geometry, so they cannot be readily adapted to design H-bond 65 networks with high connectivity or specific amino acid compositions. 66 We hypothesized that a DL approach could be used to improve the speed and accuracy of 67 H-bond network design. Here, we introduce HBDesigner, a fixed-backbone protein design 68 algorithm trained to install H-bond networks onto protein sca_olds. Our method integrates 69 recent advancements in DL based sequence design and sidechain packing with the 70 atomistic scoring capabilities of the Rosetta energy function. Results: 80 

Modeling hydrogen bond networks in silico 81 We sought an algorithm that receives a protein backbone as input and rapidly samples 82 energetically favorable H-bond networks. To achieve this, we formulated HBDesigner as a 83 three-step procedure (Fig. 1A). The first two steps use graph neural networks to encode the 84 backbone structure into a protein graph. The design model (Fig. 1A, top) autoregressively 85 predicts the position and the amino acid identities of only the network residues. This is 86 distinct from inverse folding models such as ProteinMPNN, which decode the entire 87 sequence, and it enables more rapid network sampling. The packing model (Fig. 1A, middle) 88 then predicts the sidechain chi angles for residues in the network. The third step, the scoring 89 module (Fig. 1A, bottom) , uses PyRosetta to rapidly minimize, score, and rank the predicted 90 networks. 91 HBDesigner was trained on a dataset of over 1.7 million high-quality native sidechain H-bond 92 networks curated from an August 2021 snapshot of the Protein Data Bank 18 . PyRosetta 19 was 93 used to optimize and detect networks of H-bonds with favorable geometry to use for training 94 

(Fig. 1B) . To provide fine-grained control over network design, HBDesigner was trained to use 95 di_erent types of conditioning information, which can be provided at inference time. 96 Sequence conditioning (Fig. 1C) specifies the desired amino acid composition of designed 97 networks, while virtual guide atom conditioning (Fig. 1D) specifies an approximate center-98 of-mass for the designed networks in 3D space. The model can also condition on one or 99 more existing polar residues to be used to anchor predicted networks (Fig. 1E). 100

HBDesigner improves predicted binding specificity in otherwise promiscuous sca<olds 160 

Next, we wished to test our hypothesis that installing H-bond networks into protein-protein 161 interfaces with HBDesigner could impart improved binding specificity. We collected 19 de 162 novo designed heterodimers from prior design e_orts and generated a family of 10 highly 163 similar backbones (template modeling [TM] score>0.9) for each sca_old using partial 164 RFdi_usion 21 . We designed sequences for each backbone using ProteinMPNN, with and 165 without first installing a 4-residue network across the interface using HBDesigner. We then 166 generated all possible A/B subunit pairs within each family and refolded them with AF3, 167 using the interface predicted template modeling (ipTM) confidence score as a proxy for 168 predicted binding. When designed only with ProteinMPNN, the correctly paired dimers 169 received high ipTM scores (median=0.90), but the mispaired dimers also received moderate 170 ipTMs (median=0.73), indicating only modest specificity for the designed dimer interactions 171 

(Fig. 2H, left) . When using HBDesigner, the correctly paired dimers produced only slightly 172 lower ipTMs (median=0.88), while the mispaired dimers received significantly worse scores 173 (median=0.51), indicating higher predicted specificity (Fig. 2H, right) .174

Design and characterization of de novo heterodimers with improved binding selectivity 251 

Finally, encouraged by the results of our in silico specificity test (Fig. 2 H), we designed an 252 experiment to assess whether installing H-bond networks across a designed interface could 253 impart binding specificity among otherwise promiscuous binding partners. To do this, we 254 reanalyzed the heterodimers from the in silico test to identify families of heterodimers with 255 high predicted specificity when designed with HBDesigner followed by ProteinMPNN (Fig. 256 

5B, top) and low predicted specificity when designed with ProteinMPNN alone (Fig. 5B, 257 

bottom) . From this, we identified a set of 6 homologous heterodimers (Fig. 5A) , which we 258 expressed and tested for binding using ELISA. As predicted from AF3 crossdocking, the 259 ProteinMPNN heterodimers showed high binding a_inity for both their intended design 260 partners and their o_-target partners (Fig. 5C, bottom) . The HBDesigner heterodimers, on 261 the other hand, had significantly improved specificity for their intended design partners, at 262 the cost of diminished on-target a_inity (Fig. 5C, top) . Our best-performing design, HBDes3, 263 showed 110 nM on-target a_inity with 100-fold specificity over its cognate o_-target 264 partners. These results indicate that HBDesigner and AF3 can be used to explicitly design 265 specific binding between protein subunits with highly similar folds and interface shapes. 266

Discussion 268 

This study was motivated by the hypothesis that a task-specific deep learning (DL) approach 269 to hydrogen-bonding network design could o_er advantages over both empirical methods 270 and generalist DL methods by combining aspects of each. In this work, we have 271 demonstrated that this approach, which we call HBDesigner, enables rapid design of H-bond 272 networks into arbitrary protein backbones. On an in silico benchmark of over 2000 native and 273 de novo backbones, our method produced buried H-bond networks more reliably than 274 ProteinMPNN while o_ering similar or better speed and designability compared to MC 275 HBNet. Our experimental results further validate our method by showing how HBDesigner 276 can be readily applied to common design tasks, including installation of H-bond networks 277 into protein cores and both symmetric and asymmetric interfaces. 278 We developed HBDesigner as a drop-in module intended for use in the early steps of the in 279 

silico design pipeline. In the experiments described above, we treat H-bond networks as 280 privileged motifs akin to enzyme active sites and follow installation of the network with 281 sequence design for the rest of the protein. In this setting, we observed that ProteinMPNN 282 was often able to extend networks introduced by HBDesigner (Fig. 3A and Fig. 4 C-D, tan 283 

sticks) with additional polar residues. This behavior enabled us to design large (6+ residue) 284 networks starting from seed networks of only 3-4 residues. We also observed that AF3 is 285

11 Design of polar interfaces has been recognized as a longstanding challenge in protein 322 design 27 ,28 . Based on this precedent, we expected that more polar interfaces would be more 323 di_icult to design. Indeed, we observed lower dimerization success rates for more polar 324 homodimer designs, with a 33% success rate for the top half (5 of 15), compared to 81% (13 325 of 16) for the bottom half (Fig. 4B) . Several of our designs achieved similar interface polarity 326 scores to those of the native obligate homodimers (Fig. 4B) , demonstrating that de novo 327 designed oligomers can tolerate native-like interface polarity. Most of our designs relied 328 upon parallel or antiparallel helical packing commonly found in de novo designed 329 assemblies 10,29 , while two designs used b-strand pairing to form either part (S2G) or all (S2H) 330 

of the interface (Extended Data Fig. 4) . This enabled elevated interface polarity (33% and 331 62% for S2G and S2H, respectively) despite relatively small sidechain H-bond networks. 332 However, we observed significant oligomerization of both proteins by mass photometry, 333 particularly S2H (Extended Data Fig. 4). Recent work on de novo binder design has 334 highlighted b-strand pairing as a strategy for improv ing binder specificity toward certain 335 

targets 30 , although this relies on the target having exposed b-strands with non -canonical 336 

geometry to avoid aggregation. Our approach serves as an orthogonal strategy for achieving 337 polar interfaces primarily through sidechain-sidechain interactions, which is amenable to 338 any backbone topology and therefore more generalizable. 339 Our heterodimer specificity experiment (Fig. 5) was motivated by the hypothesis that, for 340 su_iciently similar sca_olds, H-bond networks could o_er a way to impart specificity where 341 shape complementarity and nonpolar packing may fail. Our results support this premise, as 342 the HBDesigner-generated heterodimers displayed improved specificity despite sharing 343 nearly identical predicted structures (TM score>0.95) and highly similar sequences 344 (sequence identity>60%) with their nonspecific ProteinMPNN counterparts (Fig. 5A) . This is 345 consistent with prior work using MC HBNet to design specificity into coiled coil 346 assemblies 3,15,31 . We also observed reduced on-target a_inity in the HBDes series (Fig. 5C) ,347 which suggests that there may be a trade-o_ between a_inity and specificity with respect to 348 interface polarity. Consistent with this observation, prior work has found that obligate 349 homodimers typically possess more hydrophobic interfaces than transient ones 32,33 . Still, 350 given that these designs were obtained from a one-shot design e_ort, it is likely that their 351 a_inity could be improved with more extensive iterative design techniques 34,35 .352 The AF3 ipTM score and its derivatives are a commonly used proxy for binding likelihood, and 353 they have been shown to be moderately discriminative between binders and nonbinders 354 across many targets 31,34,36 . We therefore hypothesized that ipTM could be used to predict 355 specificity by screening on-target and o_-target A/B subunit pairs. Methods 383 

Dataset curation 384 

For model training and validation, we curated a modified version of the ProteinMPNN 385 dataset 16 , which is a snapshot of the Protein Data Bank from August 2021 containing only 386 protein chains with all ligands, nucleotides, solvent, and cofactor atoms removed. Biological 387 assemblies solved by x-ray crystallography or cryo-EM with resolution £ 3.5 Å and 10 -10,000 388 

total residues with ³25% of residues resolved were collected. Sidechains were decomposed 389 

into chi angles and rebuilt using idealized bond lengths and angles. PyRosetta 19 was used to 390 add and optimize polar hydrogens. Sidechain-sidechain H-bonds were calculated using the 391 Rosetta REF2015 energy function 40 and used as edges to construct an H-bond graph for each 392 assembly (Fig. 1B) . H-bond networks were identified using connected-component analysis, 393 and those with 2-6 residues were retained. To avoid transient surface networks, we kept only 394 networks for which ³50% of residues were buried (>4.0 weighted sidechain neighbors as 395 

determined by the PyRosetta LayerSelector). The curated assemblies were split chain-wise 396 into sequence clusters (30% sequence identity) following the splits from ProteinMPNN. This 397 resulted in 19,916/1,209/1,247 clusters assigned to the training/validation/test sets, 398 respectively, containing 84,388/4,003/3,872 chains with 1,767,365/98,078/84,019 H-bond 399 networks. 400 

Training procedure 401 

During training, we cycle through a shu_led list of sequence clusters and sample a random 402 assembly from each cluster, then sample a random H-bond network for each assembly. 403 Each assembly-network pair constitutes one training example. The sequence and sidechain 404 coordinates of all non-network residues are cleared prior to featurization. We train both the 405 design and packing model using PyTorch 41 for 200,000 optimizer steps using a batch size of 406 10,000 tokens. We use the Adam optimizer with the Noam learning rate schedule 42 with a 407 warmup period of 4,000 steps followed by an exponential decay for the remainder of training. 408 

Design model architecture 409 

The design model (Fig. 1A, top) is implemented as a message passing neural network 410 (MPNN) in which protein residues are represented as nodes in the molecular graph, with 411 edges connecting residues to their 30 nearest neighbors (assigned based on C b-Cb412 

distances). Geometric information derived from the backbone structure is encoded as a 413 combination of node- and edge-level features. Conditioning information influences the 414 model at each layer through conditional features. See Supplementary Table 1 for a 415 summary of all input features. 416

Scoring module implementation 520 

HBDesigner uses PyRosetta to minimize and score the predicted networks (Fig. 1A, 521 

bottom) . The MinMover class is used with a tolerance of 1e-6 and maximum of 1,000 522 

lbfgs_armijo_nonmonotone minimizer iterations. Cartesian minimization is enabled using 523 the full REF2015 score function, and all atoms are fixed except the sidechain atoms of the 524 network residues. Minimized networks are first checked for validity, meaning that all network 525 residues are participating in a single contiguous network of sidechain-sidechain H-bonds. If 526 valid, the network is scored with the metrics described in the MC HBNet paper 8, which are 527 intended to reward highly connected, energetically favorable networks. These metrics 528 include buried unsatisfied heavy polar atoms (BUHs) and buried unsatisfied polar hydrogens 529 (BUPHs), saturation, and the HBNet Score. Saturation estimates the fraction of potential H-530 bonding donor/acceptor sites being satisfied by H-bonds in a set of residues for a given 531 geometry. HBNet Score is the di_erence in energy between the minimized network and an 532 equivalent backbone with all residues replaced by glycine. 533

18 Once trained, HBDesigner can be applied to new proteins in inference mode. Given an input 535 backbone and, if applicable, conditioning information, the design model is repeatedly 536 sampled to collect many unique potential networks (typically 100-1,000 per backbone). In 537 sampling mode, the design model autoregressively assigns network residues until the 538 desired network size has been reached. At each decoding step, the position of the next 539 network residue is predicted, then its amino acid identity is assigned. On subsequent steps, 540 these decoded residues are used to update the node embeddings which are fed back into 541 the model, now representing a partially decoded network. Once fully sampled, the 542 backbones are cropped, and their network sidechains are predicted with the HBDesigner 543 packing model. They are then minimized and scored with various H-bonding metrics using 544 PyRosetta, as described above. Any predictions that fail to form contiguous networks of H-545 bonds are discarded, and additional filters (e.g., saturation) can be used to further trim the 546 set of returned networks. By default, we follow the MC HBNet convention and return only 547 networks with £0 BUHs, £5 BUPHs, ³0.5 saturation, and £0 REU HBNet Score. Networks are 548 

then sorted by these same metrics, prioritized in the order written, and the top K networks 549 are returned. As shown in Table 1 , total runtime for HBDesigner is under 10 seconds per 550 sca_old for most common use cases. 551

22 We generated de novo homodimer backbones of 60-120 residues by RFdi_usion using C2 677 symmetry and auxiliary potential olig_contacts , with intrachain and interchain weights set to 678 0.4 and 0.2, respectively. The resulting sca_olds were filtered by radius of gyration to ensure 679 backbones were compact, and any designs with entwined topologies were removed. We ran 680 HBDesigner to design two-, three-, and four-residue networks across the interface, 681 collecting the top 15 networks per sca_old. We used symmetry constraints (flag --682 

symm_chains A,B) to select only networks which could be symmetrized across the interface 683 without introducing clashes. This means that, for example, each three-residue network 684 design actually has two copies of that network including six total residues. All polar residues 685 were allowed, with a minimal burial value of 3.0 weighted sidechain neighbors in the 686 interface. 687 To design the remaining sequence, LigandMPNN was used to generate 5 sequences per 688 network, providing the HBDesigner network as sequence context. We used a sampling 689 temperature of 0.1 and --homo_oligomer flag turned on to automatically set symmetric 690 residues and equal symmetry weights. We then refolded all sequences using AlphaFold3, as 691 previously described. All successfully refolded sequences (average pLDDT ≥ 80, ipTM ≥ 0.8, 692 RMSD Cα ≤ 2 Å) were minimized with PyRosetta and filtered based on full recovery of the 693 designed H-bond network. 694 Multi-network designs were generated by collecting all single-network designs that passed 695 all design filters and attempting to graft in an additional network sampled for the same 696 backbone. To avoid steric clashes, combined networks were rejected if any sidechain heavy 697 atoms from individual networks were separated by less than 3 Å, or if any positions would be 698 occupied by both networks at once. An additional round of sequence design, refolding, and 699 filtering was then performed as described above. 700
