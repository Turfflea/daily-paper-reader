Multimodal single-cell perturbation screens offer a scalable approach for characterizing the effects of genetic and chemical interventions on cellular state. However, most existing representation-learning methods are tailored to a single perturbation modality and fail to explicitly incorporate external se-mantic knowledge, which limits their ability to generalize across datasets and perturbation types. Here, we introduce PertOmni, a CLIP-style multimodal representation-learning framework that aligns tran-scriptomic perturbation signatures with text-derived embeddings of curated genes and compound de-scriptions, as well as image-derived embeddings from cell paintings. PertOmni jointly trains a shared transcriptomic encoder and dataset-specific text encoders using a masked contrastive objective that emphasizes within–cell-type discrimination while mitigating confounding effects arising from cell-type heterogeneity. We evaluate the produced joint embedding space on bi-directional retrieval, drug–gene interaction inference, and perturbation prediction across both small-molecule and CRISPRi perturba-tion datasets, and demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baseline methods. 

Keywords: Perturb-seq, contrastive learning, multi-modality, large language model 1

1. Introduction 2

Numerous perturbation experiments have been developed to systematically quantify cellular changes 3

under varying conditions (1–4), enabling the study of how cells respond to interventions. Recent ad-4

vances in single-cell RNA sequencing enable these responses to be profiled at single-cell resolution, 5

yielding control and perturbed transcriptomic readouts across many perturbations (2, 5, 6). Broadly 6

speaking, such perturbations can be grouped into three major categories based on the perturbation 7

used. Genetic perturbations, such as CRISPR-based ones (2), leverage targeted genome editing to 8

disrupt or modulate gene function, thereby altering the expression of the perturbed gene and its 9

associated regulatory network. Small-molecule perturbations (7, 8), in contrast, use chemical com-10 

pounds that typically act on protein products such as enzymes or receptors, indirectly reshaping the 11 

transcriptional landscape of affected pathways. Biologics form another category and include, for ex-12 

ample, extracellular ligand stimulation with cytokines. Together, these experimental strategies have 13 

become important tools for understanding cellular circuitry, providing insights into fundamental 14 

biology and enabling the discovery of therapeutic targets. 15

Analyzing gene expression profiles from perturbation experiments remains challenging. Histori-16 

cally, the high cost of such experiments limited the availability of large-scale, genome-wide, or 17 

atlas-level datasets (6). Although several institutes and companies have released single-cell per-18 

turbation datasets with multiple replicates and experimental conditions (8–11), data quality and 19 

noise remain persistent concerns. Perturbations can trigger stress or cell-death programs; moreover, 20 

nuclease-based genetic perturbations can induce complex DNA damage such as chromothripsis (12), 21 

and perturbation efficacy can be heterogeneous across cells (13). In addition, single-cell RNA-seq 22 

measurements are prone to technical artifacts and batch effects (14, 15). These challenges present 23 

as obstacles in analyzing perturbation effects rigorously. 24 

In addition to transcriptomic profiling, image-based perturbation assays such as Cell Painting pro-25 

vide a complementary strategy for measuring cellular responses (16). Cell Painting uses multiplexed 26 

fluorescence microscopy to capture high-content morphological profiles across multiple cellular com-27 

partments, enabling large-scale phenotypic screening of genetic or chemical perturbations (17, 18). 28 

Compared with single-cell RNA-seq, Cell Painting offers a different view of perturbation effects by di-29 

rectly measuring cellular morphology and subcellular organization. However, linking image-derived 30 

phenotypes with textual biological knowledge and transcriptomic perturbation effects remains un-31 

derexplored, especially in settings involving both genetic and small-molecule perturbations. 32 

A range of computational methods for denoising, normalizing, and modeling perturbation responses 33 

has been proposed to address these challenges, including (19–26). These methods typically use 34 

as input a representation of the perturbation and the transcriptomic profile of the control cells of 35 

interest. More recently, several single-cell foundation models such as (27–30) have also claimed to 36 

support perturbation response prediction, but benchmarking studies such as (10, 31, 32) suggest that 37 

their generalization remains limited. scGen (33) aims to predict single-cell perturbation responses 38 

by learning a latent representation and transferring perturbation effects via latent-space arithmetic, 39 

while GPerturb (34) estimates gene-level perturbation effects with calibrated uncertainty. Despite 40 

these advances, two major gaps remain. First, most approaches overlook the integration of external 41 

biological knowledge expressed in natural language, such as functional annotations of genes and 42 

drugs, which could improve interpretability and predictive accuracy. Second, few frameworks model 43 

both genetic and chemical perturbations. Such integration would enable the inference of drug–target 44 

relationships. 45 

Therefore, we introduce PertOmni, a unified framework for modeling and bridging perturbation 46 

data across diverse perturbations and technologies. PertOmni learns a shared embedding space by 47 

contrastively aligning LLM-derived perturbation text embeddings with transcriptomic perturbation-48 

effect embeddings across genetic and small-molecule screens. Specifically, we embed curated gene 49 

and drug descriptions, such as from NCBI (35), and jointly train text and transcriptome encoders so 50 

that matched perturbations and expression readout are close in the latent space. We evaluate Per-51 

tOmni on (i) bi-directional perturbation retrieval (text to transcriptome and transcriptome to text) 52 

and (ii) downstream applications, including drug–gene interaction inference and retrieval-based per-53 

turbation prediction, demonstrating improved alignment and utility compared to strong baselines. 54

3. Discussion 152 

We introduce PertOmni, a framework for aligning representations of perturbation effects across 153 

modalities. By leveraging LLM embeddings and masked contrastive learning, PertOmni successfully 154 

integrates heterogeneous cell context and perturbation information, outperforming strong baselines 155 

across four applications relevant to drug discovery. 156 

The key innovation of PertOmni lies in two dimensions. First, PertOmni leverages the prior knowl-157 

edge introduced by databases and translates the textual descriptions into numerical representations 158 

with the help of LLM embedding layers. We also perform a joint training approach by matching 159 

the samples from sequencing data with their correct paired embeddings in different cell types, and 160 

thus we can achieve perturbation modeling at the cell-type-level resolution, thereby better align-161 

ing with biological requirements and providing more insights. Second, our approach attempts to 162 

integrate perturbation datasets from diverse sources, including genetic perturbations and chemical 163 

perturbations, thereby directly linking drugs to their potential targets. We also demonstrated that 164 

after integrated training, we can better predict drug-gene interactions, thereby providing insights 165 

for clinical applications. 166 

In addition, PertOmni simultaneously addresses multiple applications, thereby supporting its role as 167 

a unified model for studying perturbation effects. In addition to predicting drug-gene interactions, 168 

we can also enhance the correlation of predicting perturbations effects at the cellular type level 169 

under different gene groups. PertOmni also enables us to search for neighbors of specified genes 170 

based on embedding similarity, thereby enhancing the effectiveness of related tools by constructing 171 

gene similarity networks. For example, utilizing improved gene sets can help LangPert better pre-172 

dict perturbation outcomes. Additionally, PertOmni shows its superiority in multi-modality tasks, 173 

outperforming baselines in both gene-text retrieval and image-text retrieval. Our framework also 174 

employs rigorous experimental design and validation, enabling us to select the most suitable model 175 

architecture and parameters to achieve optimal performance across multiple tasks. 176 

Although PertOmni performs well across multiple datasets and tasks, it still has some limitations. 177 

First, the performance of PertOmni depends on the current LLM’s ability to integrate knowledge. 178 

Therefore, we need to track the progress of advanced LLM research to ensure we always use state-179 

of-the-art embeddings. Second, PertOmni cannot directly process other informative modalities, such 180 

as protein-based perturbation data, due to data access limitation. By leveraging these multimodal 181 

perturbation datasets, we may push the unified setting of our current design. In the future, we will 182 

focus on these areas to continue improving the boundaries of PertOmni with the help of enriched 183 

datasets. 184 

Overall, our work shows that LLM-derived priors can be combined with sequencing readouts to learn 185 

transferable perturbation representations. Future work could extend PertOmni to additional modal-186 

ities and update LLM embeddings to improve generalization with larger and more diverse data. 187

Data preparation. For small-molecule perturbations, we use the Tahoe-100M dataset (8). For ge-199 

netic perturbations, we use 5 cell lines across 3 datasets: K562 and RPE1 (40), HepG2 and Jurkat (9), 200 

and HCT116 (45). For both modalities, cells and genes are first filtered based on standard quality 201 

control criteria following the Scanpy tutorial (50), including the removal of cells that express fewer 202 

than 100 genes and the removal of genes that are detected in fewer than three cells. In each plate or 203 

batch, we perform pseudobulk aggregation by summing gene expression across cells sharing the same 204 

condition (perturbation and cell line). Then, normalization is performed following the Scanpy tuto-205 

rial (50), where we count-normalize each pseudobulk sample to the median of total counts and apply 206 

a log1p transformation. For the Tahoe-100M dataset, text descriptions for perturbations are obtained 207 

directly from the dataset, where compound descriptions are extracted from MedChemExpress (51) 208 

using Selenium(52). For CRISPRi data, embeddings for target genes are taken from GenePT (43). 209 

For both datasets, cell line text descriptions are generated from GPT-4o using the prompt: "Please 210 

summarize the information of cell line: {cell line} in one paragraph. Use academic language and in-211 

clude pathway information." All perturbation- and cell line-level text descriptions are then converted 212 

into 1536 -dimensional embeddings using the text-embedding-3-small model. We split the training, 213 

validation, and test sets into 4/1/1 based on perturbations in the two datasets, ensuring that per-214 

turbations in the test set are not observed during training. For PertOmni-chem and PertOmni-cri 215 

training, we use all genes in Tahoe-100M dataset and CRISPRi dataset, respectively. For PertOmni 216 

training, we use the intersection of genes between the two datasets. 217 

For the image-text retrieval task, we use the RxRx3-core dataset (48), a Cell Painting dataset curated 218 

from the RxRx3 dataset (18). The RxRx3-core dataset contains 222 , 601 six-channel fluorescent 219 

microscopy images of HUVEC cells, covering 736 genetic perturbations and 1, 674 compound per-220 

turbations. We follow the image preprocessing steps from CellCLIP (47), where we normalize raw 221 

Cell Painting image values into 0 − 255 . Then we treat each Cell Painting channel as an indepen-222 

dent grayscale image and extract embeddings separately using pretrained DINOv2-giant model (49). 223 

For retrieval evaluation on unseen perturbations, we split the perturbations into 70 /10 /20 for train-224 

ing, validation and testing. For the text embedding, we first extract compound descriptions from 225 

MedChemExpress (51), following Tahoe-100M (8). For compounds that are not available on Med-226 

ChemExpress, we generate compound text descriptions from GPT-4o using the prompt: "Please sum-227 

marize the information of compound perturbation: {compound} in one paragraph. Use academic 228 

language and include pathway information." Text descriptions for CRISPR perturbations and cell line 229 

are generated in the same manner. Then all text descriptions are converted into 1536 -dimensional 230 

embeddings using the text-embedding-3-small model. The perturbation embeddings and cell line 231 

embeddings are concatenated to form the text embeddings for training PertOmni. 232 

Encoder architecture. All encoders ( 𝑃 𝑋 , 𝑃 𝑍 𝑑 , 𝑃 𝑍 𝑔 ) are lightweight projection heads with two fully-233 

connected layers and a residual connection. Each maps an input of dimension 𝑑 to a 128-dimensional 234
