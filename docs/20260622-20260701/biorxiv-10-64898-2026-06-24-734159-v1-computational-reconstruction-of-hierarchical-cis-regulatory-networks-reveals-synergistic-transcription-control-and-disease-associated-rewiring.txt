Abstract 27 Gene regulation emerges from coordinated interactions among dispersed 28 cis-regulatory elements, yet how these elements integrate into functional regulatory 29 networks and collectively regulate gene transcription remains poorly understood. Here, we 30 present ORIGAMI, a multi-omics, gene-centric deep learning framework that reconstructs 31 functional cis-regulatory networks constrained by transcriptional output. ORIGAMI 32 formulates cis-regulatory modeling as a latent graph inference task, which integrates DNA 33 sequence, epigenomic signals, and three-dimensional chromatin priors to infer denoised 34 regulatory graphs that capture functional interactions rather than structural proximity 35 alone. The inferred regulatory graphs exhibit distinct topological regimes, where 36 hierarchical and modular organization encodes cell-state-specific functional demands and 37 enables synergistic transcriptional control. Furthermore, we show that these regulatory 38 architectures undergo measurable state-dependent rewiring across disease contexts. Finally, 39 ORIGAMI accurately predicts the transcriptional consequences of both cis- and 40 trans-regulatory perturbations and links the rearrangement of regulatory architecture to 41 perturbation response. Together, ORIGAMI advances a network-based view of gene 42 regulation and establishes a foundation for virtual cell modeling of regulatory dynamics. 43

Main 47 Gene regulation is fundamentally an emergent property of distributed molecular 48 interactions across multiple genomic scales, in which dispersed candidate cis-regulatory 49 elements (cCREs) collectively coordinate spatiotemporal expression of genes 1 . CREs are 50 regions of non-coding DNA which reside hundreds to millions of base pairs upstream or 51 downstream of the genes they regulate 2–5 , and variations within CREs may lead to various 52 diseases by disrupting the normal expression of target genes 6–8 . Rather than acting 53 independently, cCREs form structured and hierarchical interaction networks that integrate 54 signals across three-dimensional chromatin folding to regulate gene expression 9–11 .55 However, due to the complexity and cell type-specificity of the interactions between CREs, 56 deciphering how local and long-range regulatory grammar are integrated into gene-centric 57 cis-regulatory networks remains a central challenge in regulatory genomics 12–15 .58 Experimentally constructing cis-regulatory network suffers from low resolution and 59 high cost, while computationally reconstructing regulatory architectures requires 60 overcoming significant data sparsity and noise. Chromatin conformation capture 61 technologies such as Hi-C only measure physical proximity between genomic segments 62 rather than direct regulatory activity 16 . Regulatory activity assays such as CRISPR 63 screening 17–20 , STARR-seq 21,22 , and massively parallel reporter assays (MPRA) 23,24 , enable 64 systematic evaluation of CREs activity. However, the vast number of cCREs and their 65 context-specific activity 3 make comprehensive assessment across all elements and their 66 combinations in different cell types infeasible. Recent deep learning methods have made 67 substantial progress in inferring cis-regulatory grammar and predicting gene expression 68 from genomic and epigenomic inputs 12,19,25–31 , yet most approaches implicitly infer 69 CRE-gene interactions from attention mechanisms or gradient analysis and overlooking 70 the explicit modeling of hierarchical long-range regulation between CREs 19,25,28–30 .71 Several graph-based models have begun to incorporate 3D genome information, but they 72 often inherit noise from chromatin contact data and lack mechanisms to distinguish 73 functional interactions from structural proximity 27,31 , limiting their ability to capture 74 causally relevant regulatory relationships. 75 Here we present ORIGAMI, multiOmics-based cis-Regulatory Interaction and Gene 76 Activity Modeling with hierarchical Inference, to infer functional cis-regulatory networks 77 by explicitly linking network reconstruction to transcriptional regulation. Taking 80 multi-omics sequence profiles with three-dimensional chromatin conformation data of 81 cCREs flanking the target gene as input, ORIGAMI jointly reconstructs cis-regulatory 82 networks for each gene and predicts expression using the reconstructed network. 83 Specifically, ORIGAMI reconstructs hierarchical interactions among all the regulatory 84 elements of a target gene using sparse and noisy chromatin contact data as prior 85 interactions. This is achieved by a graph autoencoder that embeds regulatory elements into 86 a latent space, where cCREs with similar regulatory function and multi-omics features are 87 more closer, thereby eliminated noise from prior interactions. Meanwhile, the expression 88 prediction task guides and constrains model to reconstruct true regulatory interactions, 89 which means only interactions that support accurate expression prediction are retained, 90 ensuring that the learned graph captures causal and functional regulatory structure, rather 91 than merely structural chromatin proximity. 92 Leveraging data across multiple cell types, we show that the regulatory complexity of 93 networks tightly links to functional specialization of cell identity and undergoes targeted 94 rewiring during tumorigenesis. By performing in silico perturbations of cis- and 95 trans-regulatory variants, we found that ORIGAMI accurately predicts perturbation 96 responses and revealed how non-coding variations drive transcriptional reprogramming 97 through the cis-regulatory network rewiring. In a word, ORIGAMI models the hierarchical 98 organization of gene cis-regulation, from local sequence within individual cCREs to 99 long-range, gene-centric coordination among multiple regulatory elements. This 100 computational framework bridges the gap between static multi-omics measurements and 101 dynamic systems biology, providing a generalizable engine for the predictive modeling of 102 virtual cells. 103 104

ORIGAMI formulates gene regulation as function-constrained latent graph inference 106 ORIGAMI formulates the deciphering of gene regulation as a graph inference 107 problem. By integrating genomic and epigenomic profiles of cCREs, together with 108 chromatin interaction information, the framework jointly reconstruct cis-regulatory 109 interaction networks and predict downstream gene expression (Fig. 1). ORIGAMI is 110 trained using cell line-specific active cCREs identified by Encyclopedia of DNA Elements 111 (ENCODE) 3 as the fundamental units rather than whole genome sequences. First, a hierarchical transformer module 117 encodes regulatory information of cCREs and promoter flanking the target gene at two 118 levels: a CRE-centric encoder aggregates base-level genome sequence and epigenomic 119 signals into latent representation for each cCRE, while a regulatory-centric encoder 120 models long-range regulatory relationships of cCREs through genomic position-aware 121 attention mechanism. This design thereby generates the regulatory-aware embeddings that 122 contains both local regulatory grammar and gene-level regulatory coordination. 123 Second, we initialize the regulatory landscape of each target gene as a graph: 124 regulatory-aware embeddings as node features, and Hi-C-derived chromatin contacts as 125 sparse and noisy prior edges. Then, a graph autoencoder module encodes cCRE latent 126 representations by aggregating 3D genome-neighboring cCRE information and 127 reconstructs the denoised interaction probability of cCRE-cCRE and cCRE-promoter. 128 Importantly, this step transforms noisy and low-resolution chromatin contact data into 129 gene-specific cis-regulatory networks. 130 Finally, ORIGAMI constrains cis-regulatory network reconstruction task through a 131 graph regression-based gene expression prediction task. The reconstructed network is 132 hierarchically aggregated using differentiable graph pooling (DiffPool), yielding a133 graph-level gene representation which is subsequently regressed to observed expression 134 values. This coupling of two tasks ensures that only true regulatory interactions, which 135 contribute to accurate expression prediction, are retained, thereby filtering spurious 136 contacts and enhancing biological relevance. 137 In summary, by explicitly coupling latent graph reconstruction with gene expression 138 prediction, the model learns cis-regulatory networks that encode functional regulatory 139 causality rather than mere chromatin proximity, enabling mechanistic interpretation of 140 non-coding regulatory architecture. 141 142

Beyond pairwise accuracy, the reconstructed matrices recapitulated known 182 biophysical constraints of genome folding (Fig. 2E). Topologically associating domains 183 (TADs) are self-interacting genomic regions in which cis-regulatory elements 184 preferentially contact other elements within the same domain, resulting in higher 185 intra-domain contact frequencies than inter-domain interactions 33 . Consistent with this 186 topological structure, ORIGAMI captured the characteristic decay of contact probability 187 with increasing genomic distance and clear stratification between intra- and inter-TAD 188 interactions (Fig. 2F). We further leveraged previously defined K562 TAD boundaries 34 to 189 evaluate whether the model learned domain insulation behavior. As shown in Figure 2G, 190 CRE pairs that locate at the boundary of same TAD (anchor-anchor) were assigned higher 191 contact probability compared to contacts either between boundary and TAD inner region 192 (anchor-TAD internal), or unmatched boundaries spanning adjacent TADs 193 (anchor-adjacent anchor). Similarly, at a more refined level of chromatin loops, ORIGAMI 194 confers significantly higher contact probabilities for interactions within the same loop and 195 between anchors of same loop, far exceeding those for interactions spanning adjacent 196 loops (Figure 2H). These patterns indicate that ORIGAMI implicitly captures multi-scale 197 topological constraints of 3D genome organization. 198 More crucially, this reliable network reconstruction successfully translates into 199 improved gene expression prediction. ORIGAMI achieved high concordance between 200 predicted and observed expression levels on zero-shot test dataset (Pearson r = 0.84) (Fig. 201 2I-J). Compared with four retrained state-of-the-art baselines, ORIGAMI consistently 202 achieved the best performance across multiple metrics (Fig. 2K). Together, these findings 203 demonstrate that ORIGAMI reconstructs functionally-grounded regulatory hierarchies 204 rather than isolated enhancer-gene pairs, thus bridging noncoding genome organization 205 with transcriptional output. 206 207

Applying the transformation pipeline to the reconstructed cis-regulatory interaction 216 matrices, we built 11,596 cis-regulatory networks for protein-coding genes in the 217 autosomes of K562. We observed that the networks exhibit markedly different degrees of 218 complexity, ranging from the simplest networks containing an isolated promoter to the 219 most complex network comprising 93 tightly interconnected regulatory elements ( PFAS ,220 Fig. 3B). Therefore, we classified these cis-regulatory networks into simple, sparse, and 221 complex modes based on network size and edges (Fig. 3B), and found that network 222 complexity reflects distinct regulatory demands, even for members of the same protein 223 family. For example, among STAT family members in K562, STAT6 was governed by a 224 simple network consisted of a promoter connected to less than 3 nearby CREs; STAT2 and 225 

STAT3 displayed sparse networks with more CREs but limited connectivity; whereas 226 

STAT5A and STAT5B were regulated by complex networks with dense interactions (Fig. 227 3C). Given the central role of STAT5 in sustaining leukemic survival and proliferation 35 ,228 the complex architectures of STAT5A and STAT5B suggest that disease-relevant STAT 229 members require coordinated cis-regulatory control in K562. More broadly, these results 230 indicate that functional divergence within a protein family 36 may be accompanied by 231 divergence in cis-regulatory architecture. 232 Extending the analysis to all K562 cis-regulatory networks further showed that the 233 increased network complexity was accompanied by coordinated changes in topological 234 properties, including enhanced transitivity and elevated average clustering coefficient (Fig. 235 3D). These features indicate dense communities or “small-world” properties emerging in 236 the complex network, suggesting that complex networks are structured for hierarchical 237 regulatory control. These unique properties of the network functionally manifest as genes 238 regulated by complex cis-regulatory networks exhibiting significantly higher expression 239 levels than those regulated by simple or sparse networks (Fig. 3E). Moreover, genes under 240 the regulation of complex networks are preferentially enriched for cell identity markers, 241 chronic myeloid leukemia-associated disease genes, while their constituent CREs showed 242 higher enrichment in GWAS SNPs associated with blood traits (Fig. 3F). 243 244

Modularity specificity acts as a topological signature of cell identity 284 Building on the observation that modular topology of regulatory network provides 285 transcriptional robustness in K562, we next asked whether the deployment, and conversely, 286 absence, of such architecture encodes specific functional requirements of a given cell type 287 or disease state. To test this hypothesis, we reconstructed the cis-regulatory networks for 288 all genes across nine cell lines. Through cross-cell line comparisons, we identified genes 289 possessing modular cis-regulatory networks only in K562 (Specific modular genes), genes 290 displaying non-modular networks only in K562 (Specific non-modular genes), genes 291 possessing modular networks across all cell lines (Common modular gene), and genes 292 possessing non-modular networks across all cell lines (Common non-modular gene). 293 Utilizing these four special gene sets, we first investigated whether the specificity of 294 cis-regulatory network modularity is associated with cell-type-specific gene regulation. In 295 K562, specific modular genes were significantly enriched in K562-specific up-regulated 296 genes, whereas specific non-modular genes preferentially overlapped with K562-specific 297 down-regulated genes (Fig. 4A). This asymmetric association suggests that the emergence 298 or disappearance of modular regulatory organization is not merely a structural property, 299 but is functionally coupled to active or repressive transcriptional programs defining cell 300 identity. 301 Therefore, we hypothesized that cell line-specific gene programs can be reflected 302 through modular regulatory architectures, while non-modular networks may support 303 transcriptional repression or basal regulation, and that transitions between these 304 architectures reflect regulatory rewiring during different states. Consistent with this notion, 305 functional enrichment analysis 40 revealed a clear partition of biological roles across 306 modularity categories (Fig. 4B). K562-specific modular genes were enriched for pathways 307 underlying hematopoietic lineage and oncogenic drivers associated with leukemia, 308 representing an “acquired identity” unique to the lineage-specific and malignant state of 309 K562. Conversely, K562-specific non-modular genes were enriched for pathways 310 underlying immune response and cellular senescence, reflecting the contextual shutdown 311 of relevant transcriptional programs to escape survival pressures, representing a “lost 312 identity”. We further observed that conserved modularity patterns were associated with the 313 consistent survival requirements across multiple cell types. For example, genes with 314 common modular structures across diverse cell types (common module) were those 315 ubiquitously high-expressed genes. State-dependent rewiring of regulatory topology captures disease-associated 325 

reprogramming 326 After confirming the association between modularity specificity and cell identity, we 327 further exploited this characteristic to examine how network modularity is rewired during 328 disease progression. Specifically, we systematically analyzed the gain and loss of network 329 modularity during oncogenic transformation from mammary epithelial to MCF-7. A 330 substantial fraction of cis-regulatory network gained (10.2%) or lost (14.6%) modular 331 organization during malignant transformation (Fig. 4C). 332 To quantify the magnitude of network dynamics between disease and healthy states, 333 we defined a network dynamic score Scor  dynamic  which measures global changes in 334 interaction strength. Genes undergo regulatory complexity expansion have Scor  dynamic 335 larger than zero, and vice versa. Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) 41 based on 336 network dynamic scores revealed that genes gaining complexity in MCF-7 were strongly 337 associated with breast cancer-related pathways, including estrogen response and 338 oncogenic signaling, whereas genes losing complexity were associated with antigen 339 presentation pathway, suggesting a collapse of CREs coordination that leads to 340 transcriptional silencing of immune-related programs during tumor progression (Fig. 4D). 341 These results suggest that disease-associated regulatory reprogramming is accompanied by 342 systematic remodeling of network hierarchy. 343

Discussion 490 A central challenge in regulatory genomics is to bridge static, noisy measurements of 491 chromatin state with the dynamic, causal control of gene expression 25,39,49,50,53,56 . Here, we 492 formalize this problem as a latent graph inference task. By constraining network 493 reconstruction with gene expression prediction, ORIGAMI learns regulatory architectures 494 that capture both high-order genome organization and functional causality, rather than 495 structural proximity alone. 496 One key insight emerging from this work is that regulatory topological regimes is not 497 uniformly distributed across the genes, but instead reflects tissue-specific functional 498 demands. The hierarchically organized, modular regulatory architectures govern robust 499 and highly expressed genes, whereas simpler, non-modular networks are associated with 500 fundamental and low-cost regulation. Within these regimes, regulatory modules do not act 501 independently; instead, hub CREs mediate inter-module coupling, enabling non-linear 502 integration of regulatory signals 12,29,39,48,50 . Therefore, such hierarchical topology confers 503 both robustness against local perturbations and flexibility for coordinated regulatory 504 responses. 505 The dynamic rewiring of regulatory modularity across cell states further underscores 506 modular topology as a regulatory strategy rather than a static feature 15,57 . In this context, 507 tumorigenesis can be viewed as a systematic topological transition of cis-regulatory 508 networks, which is characterized by network-scale rewiring dynamics, rather than isolated 509 molecular alterations. Such transitions reshape the flow of regulatory information, 510 amplifying oncogenic programs while attenuating tumor-suppressive pathways. These 511 observations imply that pathological states may exploit regulatory rewiring to achieve 512 stable yet aberrant expression programs, offering a new perspective on how regulatory 513 networks are repurposed during tumorigenesis. 514 A particularly notable aspect of ORIGAMI is its ability to accurately predict the 515 transcriptional effects of both cis- and trans-regulatory perturbations, positioning the 516 model as a simulation-ready regulatory system. This capability aligns closely with the 517 emerging paradigm of virtual cell modeling 58–60 , which enable systematic, in silico 518

First, 524 ORIGAMI infers regulatory interactions from population-averaged bulk data, and 525 therefore does not explicitly capture continuous cell state variation. Extending this 526 framework to single-cell multi-omics data may enable mapping of continuous regulatory 527 state-space trajectories and improve prediction of heterogeneous perturbation responses 528 within microenvironments. Integrating gene-centric networks into gene regulatory networks 531 that incorporate trans-regulatory information may enable simulation of full-scale cellular 532 dynamics, advancing toward more comprehensive and authentic regulatory circuits. 533 Looking forward, ORIGAMI establishes a generalizable computational framework 534 for modeling gene regulation as a structured, multi-scale, and perturbable system, which 535 provides a foundation for predictive modeling of regulatory variation and 536 disease-associated network reprogramming. Future work may extend this framework for 537 variant interpretation, disease modeling, and the rational design of interested perturbation 538 experiments. More broadly, it advances the development of virtual cell models that enable 539 mechanistic and quantitative exploration of cellular regulation in silico. 540 541
