Abstract 

Accurate high-throughput prediction of protein-protein interactions (PPIs) is essential for mapping cellular mechanisms and prioritizing experimental valida-tion. Current graph-based methods often rely on discrete message passing, suffer from representation over-smoothing, and provide poorly calibrated confidence scores. We present LNGCN, a distance-aware continuous-time graph protocol that integrates residue-level structural graphs with liquid neural dynamics to model spatially heterogeneous interaction patterns. Residue radial distance is used as an explicit driving signal for continuous graph evolution, while hier-archical calibration converts raw model outputs into interpretable interaction probabilities. Across balanced, highly imbalanced, and cross-species benchmarks, LNGCN achieved robust predictive performance. Importantly, the calibrated scores supported biologically coherent prioritization in the FGF23-FGFR1c-α-Klotho complex, SHP2-associated signaling interactions and Tdk1 oligomeric-state-dependent binding. In a TPR-centered experimental case study, LNGCN recovered known TPR-associated partners and prioritized ELAVL1-TPR and RALY-TPR, whose physical interactions were subsequently confirmed via exper-imental validation. These results indicate that LNGCN can serve as a practical prioritization protocol for PPI candidates.  

> Keywords: High-throughput Protein-protein interactions, liquid neural network, graph convolution network

# 1 Introduction 

Protein-protein interactions (PPIs) underlie most cellular processes and provide essen-tial entry points for understanding disease mechanisms, pathway regulation and therapeutic intervention [1]. Although experimental methods remain the gold standard for PPI identification, they face significant limitations including high costs, limited throughput, and high false-positive and false-negative rates, making computational prioritization increasingly important. In practical screening settings, the central chal-lenge is not only to classify whether two proteins interact, but also to rank large candidate lists in a way that helps experimental researchers decide which interactions should be tested first. Graph-based learning has become a natural framework for PPI prediction because proteins and their structural or interaction contexts can be represented as graphs. In structure-informed models, protein three-dimensional conformations can be converted into residue-level graphs, enabling graph convolutional networks (GCNs) to capture spatially organized molecular information [5]. GCN-based architectures have there-fore been widely adopted in biomolecular prediction tasks [6]. For example, Yuan et al. applied GCNs to identify residues associated with PPIs [7], Jha et al. combined GCNs with graph attention networks for PPI prediction [8], Torng et al. used GCNs for protein-ligand interaction prediction [9], and Zhai et al. introduced an edge-aware 2

GCN framework with self-attention modules [10]. These studies demonstrate the ver-satility of graph learning, but also highlight the need for frameworks that can translate structural representations into experimentally actionable PPI scores. Despite significant progress, existing models face critical bottlenecks. First, multi-modal sequence-structure features lack efficient fusion schemes to eliminate redundant noise and capture non-linear molecular relationships [11]. Second, current GCN models employ discrete hierarchical stacking, which is inadequate for modeling the contin-uous spatial hierarchy of residues. GCN receptive fields are determined by discrete hop counts, while residue spatial positions are inherently continuous variables, causing homogenization of residues at different spatial locations and flattening critical spa-tial information. Simply extending a residual-connected GCN to a continuous-time ODE framework does not fully resolve this issue, as the driving term lacks explicit residue spatial position dependency and adaptive decay mechanisms. Third, uncali-brated raw scores cannot serve as reliable confidence metrics for candidate ranking, uncertainty evaluation and experimental thresholding. Researchers need interpretable confidence estimates that support candidate ranking, uncertainty assessment and threshold selection [12]. Continuous-time neural dynamics offer a possible route to address these issues. Liq-uid Neural Networks (LNNs) [13] and Closed-form Continuous-Time (CfC) Networks [14] provide a framework for continuous-time differential equations with learnable temporal dynamics. These architectures are attractive for structure-informed PPI screening because residue-specific spatial information can be incorporated into the driving terms of a continuous graph evolution process. In this setting, we embed residue radial distance alongside graph neighborhood aggregation into ODE driving terms. This enables distance-aware residue evolution, breaking the limitation of discrete graph layers that ignore continuous spatial distributions. Here we introduce LNGCN, a calibrated structure-aware protocol for high-throughput PPI screening and candidate prioritization. It first uses CfC preprocessing [14] to compress residue features while retaining radial distance as a spatial signal. Then LNGCN embeds graph convolution into an LNN-based continuous dynamical system [13], where neighborhood information and radial distance jointly drive residue-state evolution. Multi-scale graph convolutions capture structural patterns at different receptive fields, and a symmetric fusion module combines two protein-level representa-tions to preserve pair-order invariance. Finally, a hierarchical ensemble of Platt [17, 18], Beta [19, 20], Isotonic [18, 21, 22] and Boosting calibrators [18, 23, 24] converts raw outputs into interpretable interaction probabilities. By integrating feature compression, distance-aware continuous graph evolution, symmetric pairwise representation and probability calibration, LNGCN is designed not simply as a binary classifier but as an end-to-end screening workflow. We evaluate the protocol across balanced, imbalanced and cross-species PPI settings, and further assess whether calibrated predictions can support biologically coherent interpretation and experimental prioritization. This framing positions LNGCN as a practical tool for moving from large candidate PPI lists to ranked, testable hypotheses. 3

## 2.2 Datasets 

For model training, high-confidence PPIs with STRING scores > 0.9 were selected as positive samples from the STRING database [25]. Negative samples are generated by randomly matching from all the known protein interaction pairs to create protein pairs that do not exist in the interactions. Protein sequences and AlphaFold-predicted structures [2] were obtained from UniRef [26]. The main dataset was divided into training, validation and independent test sets, as summarized in Table 1. To evaluate robustness under realistic class imbalance, we used an independent 1:10 human PPI dataset from Zhang et al. [27], including 3000 high-confidence PPIs intersecting STRING [25], UniRef [26], and BioGrid [28], together with 29,724 negative pairs. Cross-species generalization was assessed using yeast PPI data from STRING [25]. For probability calibration, we constructed a balanced calibration dataset contain-ing 3000 positives from Zhang et al. [27] and 3000 negatives, including 2000 randomly sampled non-interacting pairs and 1000 pairs from Negatome [29]. Detailed dataset construction and filtering procedures are provided in Supplementary Information S4.

## 2.8 Candidate ranking and interpretation of calibrated probabilities 

For downstream screening, the calibrated LNGCN score was used as an empirical pri-oritization score rather than a direct biophysical binding probability or a definitive experimental success rate. Calibration was applied to reduce over-confident raw out-puts and to align model scores with the empirical label distribution of the calibration set, thereby making score ranges more suitable for threshold-based candidate selec-tion. Given a candidate protein-pair list, each pair was processed independently by the trained model, and the calibrated interaction probability was used as the primary ranking score for experimental follow-up. Candidate pairs were ranked in descending order of calibrated probability. Follow-ing the confidence intervals commonly used in STRING-based interaction interpre-tation [25], LNGCN scores were divided into five practical priority groups: 0 − 0.15 (extremely low), 0 .15 − 0.4 (low), 0 .4 − 0.7 (medium), 0 .7 − 0.9 (high), and 0 .9 − 1.0(extremely high). High-priority candidates were considered suitable for direct valida-tion, whereas medium-priority candidates were interpreted together with orthogonal evidence, such as mass spectrometry abundance, co-expression, subcellular localiza-tion or pathway context. Low-priority candidates were retained mainly as negative or background controls. This strategy enables LNGCN to transform large candidate PPI lists into experimentally testable hypotheses with interpretable confidence estimates.

## 3.2 Component contribution and calibrated probability estimation 

We performed ablation experiments to determine whether the main architectural com-ponents contributed to robust candidate scoring. Five variants were evaluated by removing: (A) the CfC preprocessing module, (B) the LNN module from the distance-aware graph driver, (C) the LNN dense layer, (D) the compensation mechanisms, and (E) both the CfC and LNN components. On the human benchmark, removing continuous-time components reduced perfor-mance (Figure 3a). The full model achieved an MCC of 0 .8936, whereas removing CfC reduced MCC by approximately 6%, removing the graph-driven LNN module reduced it by approximately 8%, and removing both CfC and LNN reduced it by approximately 12%. Cross-species ablation on yeast showed a similar trend (Figure 3b), indicat-ing that feature compression and continuous-time graph evolution jointly support transferable PPI representations. Quantitative component analysis further supported this interpretation. On the human test set, the estimated AUROC contribution was approximately 36% for CfC, 54% for the LNN module, 7% for the LNN dense layer and 3% for compensation mechanisms (Figure 3c). A comparable pattern was observed on yeast (Figure 3d). Thus, the graph-driven LNN module contributed most strongly, while CfC provided an important preprocessing step for compressing high-dimensional residue features without discarding the radial distance signal. Because LNGCN is intended for candidate ranking rather than only binary classifi-cation, we next assessed probability calibration. A calibration dataset containing 3,000 positive and 3,000 negative samples [27, 29] was constructed, and Platt [17, 18], Beta [19, 20], Isotonic [18, 21, 22] and Boosting [18, 23, 24] calibration methods were eval-uated under five-fold cross-validation. All calibration strategies maintained AUROC values above 0 .8971 and AUPRC values above 0 .8486 (Figure 3e). Isotonic [18, 21, 22] and Boosting [18, 23, 24] calibration achieved low expected calibration error (ECE), Beta calibration [19, 20] showed strong recall (0 .9257 ± 0.0364), and Platt calibration [17, 18] achieved the highest AUROC (0 .9142 ±0.0113) and AUPRC (0 .8770 ±0.0178). Since no single method was optimal across all criteria, LNGCN used hierarchical ensemble calibration to generate final interaction probabilities for confidence-guided candidate prioritization. 13

## 3.4 Experimental prioritization of TPR-associated interactions 

Finally, we evaluated whether LNGCN could support target-centered experimental candidate prioritization. # 4 Discussion and Conclusion 

In this study, we developed LNGCN, a distance-aware continuous-time graph protocol for high-throughput PPI prediction and prioritization of experimental candidates. This workflow combines multimodal sequence-structure feature extraction, continuous-time residue graph modeling, and hierarchical probability calibration to generate effec-tive interaction scores that can be used for PPI screening. This positioning is crucial 17 

> .CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 25, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.30.721835 doi: bioRxiv preprint

because large-scale PPI screening not only requires accurate discrimination between interacting and non-interacting pairs, but also requires interpretable scores to guide experimental screening. The core module of LNGCN addresses a key limitation of conventional graph-based PPI models: their reliance on discrete neighborhood aggregation, which can blur residue-level spatial heterogeneity and promote over-smoothing in deeper layers. To preserve structural hierarchy, LNGCN couples graph convolutional neighborhood information with residue radial distance as node-specific driving signals in an LNN-ODE system. This design allows each residue representation to evolve continuously under both local topology and its spatial position within the protein, thereby inte-grating sequence- and structure-derived features while maintaining spatially distinct residue states. Before graph evolution, the CfC-based preprocessing module com-presses high-dimensional residue descriptors while preserving critical radial spatial signals. After prediction, a hierarchical ensemble calibration module converts raw outputs into interpretable interaction probabilities for confidence-based candidate ranking. Benchmark results validate the framework’s robustness. On human balanced datasets, LNGCN achieved AUROC and AUPRC over 0 .98 with low cross-fold varia-tion. Under a highly imbalanced 1 : 10 setting, it maintained strong recall and AUPRC, suited for real PPI networks. Cross-species tests on yeast yielded AUPRC and AUROC values above 0 .90, proving generalized interaction pattern learning. Ablation exper-iments confirmed CfC and LNN modules deliver complementary performance gains in feature compression and continuous structural representation. A core strength lies in its integrated calibration pipeline. Raw model scores lack quantitative biological meaning, but hierarchical ensemble calibration produces more interpretable interac-tion probabilities. This makes the framework suitable not only for large-scale screening, but also for confidence-guided ranking of candidate PPIs before experiment validation. The biological case studies highlight the protocol’s practical utility. First, LNGCN delivers mechanism-informed relative ranking: it distinguishes weak direct FGFR1–FGF23 binding from strong α-Klotho-mediated interactions in the ternary hormone-signaling complex, consistent with known assembly mechanisms. Second, most medium- or higher-scoring SHP2 signaling pairs align with published literature, reflecting coherent scoring for PTM-related interactions. Third, its state-dependent interaction propensity ranking accurately captures oligomer-driven binding shifts in yeast Tdk1-Bdf1/Bdf2, showing sensitivity to high-order protein structures. Most importantly, LNGCN prioritizes TPR interactors from mass spectrometry data: its high-confidence hits are experimentally validated, and medium-ranked ELAVL1-TPR, RALY-TPR interactions are also confirmed in co-IP assays. Collectively, LNGCN acts as a reliable computational tool for large-scale PPI prediction and experimental candidate prioritization. Several limitations remain. First, the current framework focuses on binary inter-action prediction and does not explicitly model condition-specific factors such as post-translational modification state, subcellular localization or disease context. Second, cross-species validation was performed only on yeast, and broader evalua-tion across additional organisms will be needed to establish generalizability. Third, 18

Future extensions could incorporate molec-ular dynamics trajectories, where each conformational frame is represented as a time-indexed residue graph and the driving signal depends on instantaneous residue position. Such an extension may allow LNGCN to model conformationally regulated PPIs more realistically while narrowing the experimental search space for dynamic interaction mechanisms. Overall, LNGCN offers a scalable, interpretable protocol for structure-based PPI prediction and calibrated candidate prioritization. Its combination of benchmark performance, cross-species robustness and experiment-supported discovery suggests that continuous-time graph learning can be a useful strategy for computational PPI screening and mechanism-oriented biological investigation.
