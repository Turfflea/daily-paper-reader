# Abstract  

> 18

Action selection involves two systems: a model-free reinforcement learning strategy,  

> 19

which relies on experience with action–outcome pairs, and a model-based reinforcement learning  

> 20

strategy, which enables more flexible behavior via inference using a model of the invariant  

> 21

environmental structure. Although environmental change requires more flexible behavior, the  

> 22

ability of volatility, a higher-order statistic that captures how rapidly or frequently the environment  

> 23

changes, to systematically modulate these strategies remains unclear. We examined the effects  

> 24

of reward volatility on arbitration between model-free and model-based reinforcement learning  

> 25

strategies using two modified two-step decision tasks. In Experiment 1, participants performed  

> 26

tasks with different levels of reward volatility and time pressure. In Experiment 2, we  

> 27

systematically manipulated reward volatility across a broader range to assess the relationship  

> 28

between volatility and learning strategy. Behavioral data were analyzed using model-agnostic one- 

> 29

trial and multitrial back analyses, reinforcement learning simulations, and hierarchical Bayesian  

> 30

model fitting. Across experiments, reward volatility exerted an inverse U-shaped nonlinear effect  

> 31

on the arbitration between model-free and model-based reinforcement learning strategies, as the  

> 32

model-based learning strategy was strongly driven at intermediate levels of reward volatility. These  

> 33

modulation effects were observed only in individuals who had learned the transition structure in  

> 34

the task, whereas those who had not learned the transition structure relied on the model-free  

> 35

learning strategy regardless of reward volatility. Reinforcement learning simulations revealed that  

> 36

the relative advantage of the model-based learning strategy over the model-free learning strategy  

> 37

peaked at intermediate levels of reward volatility. Additionally, increased time pressure shifted  

> 38

behavior toward the model-free learning strategy. These results demonstrated that, humans do  

> 39

not always use the model-based reinforcement learning strategy in uncertain and dynamic  

> 40

environments, even when they are aware of the task structure, supporting cost–benefit  

> 41

optimization.  

> 42

# Author Summary 3 

> 43

The ability to flexibly guide behavior by carefully considering future consequences is  

> 44

fundamental to a prominent property of human intelligence and rationality. However, what drives  

> 45

this deliberative system? In this study, we investigated the factors that promote deliberative versus  

> 46

habitual behavior using decision-making tasks with uncertain structures and changing rewards. We  

> 47

found that participants who spontaneously learned the hidden transition structure in the task used  

> 48

this knowledge to guide deliberative behavior. Conversely, participants who did not learn the  

> 49

structure relied primarily on habitual strategies, repeating actions that had previously been  

> 50

rewarded. Among participants who learned the structure, the degree of deliberative behavior  

> 51

changed nonlinearly with reward volatility, in which the speed at which rewards changed over  

> 52

time. We also observed that limiting the decision time reduced deliberative behavior and promoted  

> 53

habitual responding. These findings suggest that under uncertain and dynamic environments,  

> 54

deliberative control is adaptively regulated according to cost–benefit optimization. Our results  

> 55

contribute to understanding how humans flexibly adjust their behavioral control systems in  

> 56

response to environmental conditions.

# Introduction  

> 58

Psychology, neuroscience, and behavioral economics have established that multiple  

> 59

systems control behaviors [1-11]. One system is habitual, requiring little effort and enabling rapid  

> 60

action selection, despite its sometimes inaccurate decisions. The other system is deliberate,  

> 61

requiring greater effort and more time to guide action selection but yielding more accurate  

> 62

decisions. These systems’ differing characteristics raise the following question: How do animals  

> 63

switch between them? Furthermore, what type of optimization does this switching process  

> 64

achieve?  

> 65

In recent years, several studies have investigated these questions using the framework of 4 

> 66

reinforcement learning (RL) [12-14], a formalization of the learning problem of action selection  

> 67

that maximizes reward from experience with state, action, and reward histories. Model-free (MF)- 

> 68

RL strategies correspond to the habitual systems, guided by the direct experience of actions that  

> 69

are followed by rewards, in accordance with the law of effect [15]. Conversely, model-based  

> 70

(MB)-RL strategies correspond to deliberative systems guided by state-transition probabilities that  

> 71

are analogous to a cognitive map [16]. The optimal actions are inferred according to the state- 

> 72

transition map.  

> 73

Several theoretical studies have proposed mechanisms for arbitration between MF-RL and  

> 74

MB-RL strategies, but they emphasized different computational dimensions, such as uncertainty  

> 75

in value prediction [17, 18], speed–accuracy tradeoffs [19], cost–benefit tradeoffs [20, 21], and  

> 76

conflict between MF and MB value estimates [22]. Although these factors have often been treated  

> 77

as separate determinants of arbitration, they might be jointly shaped by a more fundamental  

> 78

property of the environment: the degree to which state transitions and reward contingencies change  

> 79

over time. From this perspective, environmental volatility could provide a unifying axis for  

> 80

understanding when cached values are sufficient for efficient choice and when prospective MB  

> 81

evaluation becomes necessary.  

> 82

When observations are drawn from a probabilistic distribution, uncertainty can be  

> 83

decomposed into stochasticity, which describes variability in observations around a stable  

> 84

distribution, and volatility, which denotes changes in the distribution itself over time [23, 24]. This  

> 85

distinction is important because stochasticity and volatility impose different computational  

> 86

demands. Stochasticity limits the reliability of individual observations, whereas volatility requires  

> 87

agents to detect changes in environmental structure and update their beliefs, values, or policies  

> 88

accordingly. Both forms of uncertainty influence perception, motor control, learning, decision- 

> 89

making, and cognitive control [24-32]. 5 

> 90

In particular, volatility is particularly relevant to arbitration between MF-RL and MB-RL  

> 91

strategies because changes in environmental contingencies can require the inhibition of previously  

> 92

optimal actions and the acquisition of new optimal actions [29, 33]. MB-RL is closely linked to  

> 93

such adaptive processes, which are believed to be supported by cognitive control and working  

> 94

memory. Specifically, individual differences in cognitive control predict the extent of MB choice,  

> 95

working memory disruption selectively impairs MB but not MF control, and stress or dorsolateral  

> 96

prefrontal cortex perturbation reduces MB-RL, particularly in individuals with lower working  

> 97

memory capacity [34-37]. These findings suggest that MB-RL depends on prefrontal control  

> 98

mechanisms that maintain and update task-relevant information to adapt to environmental changes.  

> 99

At the computational level, MF-RL and MB-RL differ in their ability to efficiently adapt to  

> 100

changes in reward contingencies. MF-RL updates action values only through direct experience,  

> 101

whereas MB-RL can update action values more efficiently by combining reward information with  

> 102

an internal model of state transitions. Thus, reward volatility should modulate the relative  

> 103

advantage of MB-RL over MF-RL by changing the necessity of flexible model-based updating  

> 104

[20, 38, 39].  

> 105

If reward volatility influences arbitration between MF-RL and MB-RL through their  

> 106

differential adaptability to changing reward contingency [20, 38], its effect on MF–MB balance  

> 107

might be nonlinear (Fig 1). At low volatility, cached MF values could be sufficient for efficient  

> 108

choice, making costly MB-inferred evaluation unnecessary. At high-volatility, reward  

> 109

contingencies might change too rapidly for an internal model to generate reliable prospective  

> 110

predictions, thereby reducing the relative advantage of MB-RL. MB-RL might therefore be most  

> 111

advantageous at intermediate levels of volatility, for which MF-RL is too slow to adapt through  

> 112

direct experience alone but the environment remains sufficiently structured for MB updating to be  

> 113

useful. 6 

> 114

However, this possibility has not been tested directly. Although Kool et al. manipulated  

> 115

reward volatility, they also varied multiple other factors, making it difficult to isolate the direct  

> 116

effect of volatility [27]. Simon and Daw independently manipulated stochasticity and volatility in  

> 117

rewards, but they did not systematically examine the high-volatility regime in which both MF-RL  

> 118

and MB-RL strategies might lose their adaptive advantage [42]. Thus, whether reward volatility  

> 119

produces a nonlinear shift in MF–MB arbitration remains an open question.  

> 120

In this study, we systematically examined how reward volatility shapes arbitration  

> 121

between MF-RL and MB-RL strategies across two behavioral experiments. In Experiment 1, we  

> 122

initially tested whether reward volatility modulates the balance between MF and MB control in  

> 123

our task. We further examined whether MB-RL entails greater computational costs than MF-RL  

> 124

by manipulating time pressure during choice. In Experiment 2, we extended this approach to assess  

> 125

whether the relationship between reward volatility and MF–MB arbitration is nonlinear, as  

> 126

predicted by the differential adaptability of MF-RL and MB-RL under switching reward  

> 127

contingencies.

> 134

> 135

> 136

> 137

leading to increased engagement of the latter strategy. 7

# Effect of reward volatility and time pressure on MF–MB arbitration

# (Experiment 1)  

> 141

# Task structure  

> 142

We required 22 participants to perform a modified version of the two-step task (Fig 2A)  

> 143

[8]. Each trial featured two steps. In the first step, participants chose a black or white spaceship.  

> 144

After the choice, they probabilistically transitioned to the human or octopus state in the second  

> 145

step. Choosing the black spaceship led to the human state with 80% probability (the common  

> 146

transition) and the octopus state with 20% probability (the rare transition), whereas choosing the  

> 147

white spaceship led to the octopus state with 80% probability and the human state with 20%  

> 148

probability. In the second step, participants received rewards based on their choices. Because the  

> 149

reward magnitude for each action in the second step was independently updated by a Gaussian  

> 150

random walk with zero mean, participants had to continue learning the choice probability to  

> 151

maximize rewards. The MF-RL and MB-RL strategies could be distinguished behaviorally in the  

> 152

trial with the rare transition. After a rare transition followed by a large reward, the MF-RL strategy  

> 153

reinforced only the action chosen on that trial, whereas the MB-RL strategy reinforced the  

> 154

unchosen action more than the chosen action based on the inference that the unchosen action is  

> 155

more likely to lead to the state that is currently yielding a large reward.  

> 156

We manipulated the reward volatility as a within-participant factor (Fig 2B) defined by the  

> 157

standard deviation (SD) of the Gaussian random walk, which governs how much the reward  

> 158

changes from trial to trial. For exploratory purposes, we manipulated the complexity of the state–  

> 159

action space, defined by the number of available actions at the second step, only at a moderate 8 

> 160

level of reward volatility. Accordingly, four conditions were mixed as blocks: SD = 0.4 with one  

> 161

action (low volatility and one action; L1); SD = 1.1 with one action (moderate volatility and one  

> 162

action; M1); SD = 1.8 with one action (high volatility and one action; H1); and SD = 1.1 with two  

> 163

actions (moderate volatility and two actions; M2). Each block contained 60 trials. The available  

> 164

action at the second step was randomly chosen and fixed for each block in the L1, M1, and H1  

> 165

conditions.  

> 166

Participants completed each condition twice during the experiment. To control for order  

> 167

and interference effects, we constructed four condition sequences based on a Latin square design  

> 168

(Table 1) [40]. Order effects were counterbalanced by ensuring that all conditions appeared in each  

> 169

column of the Latin square. Interference effects were minimized by restricting the number of  

> 170

occurrences of each possible pair of consecutively presented conditions to two or three. Each  

> 171

participant performed the task across one of the four sequences.  

> 172

Additionally, we manipulated time pressure, defined by the intertrial interval (ITI), as a  

> 173

between-participants factor (Fig 2C). Because participants had to make choices at both the first  

> 174

and second steps within 1.5 s of stimulus presentation, a shorter ITI reduced the maximum time  

> 175

available for decision-making. Specifically, participants were divided into two conditions: high  

> 176

time pressure (ITI = 250 ms) and low time pressure (ITI = 500 ms). The ITI was fixed throughout  

> 177

the experiment.  

> 178

Participants were instructed in advance that each choice would be more likely to lead to  

> 179

one of the second-step states and that the reward magnitude could change over time. However,  

> 180

they were not instructed which choice was more likely to lead to which state, the exact values of  

> 181

the state-transition probabilities, the rules governing reward changes, or the underlying volatility  

> 182

conditions. After the task instructions, participants completed 20 practice trials using stimulus sets  

> 183

different from those in the main trials and then performed 480 main trials. Finally, participants 9 

> 184

answered questions, including verbal reports of the state-transition probabilities.

# Data selection  

> 201

Because learning strategies might differ fundamentally depending on whether participants  

> 202

are aware of the bias in state-transition probabilities, we selected participants and trials for analysis  

> 203

as follows. At the participant level, individuals were divided into an Aware group ( N = 18),  

> 204

consisting of those who answered “yes” to the verbal report about whether they noticed a bias in  

> 205

the state-transition probabilities, and an Unaware group ( N = 4) comprising all remaining  

> 206

participants. We focused our primary analyses on the Aware group, which had a larger sample  

> 207

size. Additionally, even participants in the Aware group likely became aware of the transition  

> 208

structure only after some experience during task performance. Although the exact point in time at  

> 209

which participants became aware could not be identified, we theoretically calculated the minimum  

> 210

number of trials required for differences in transition probabilities between actions to become  

> 211

detectable (see Methods). Consequently, the first 20 trials from the beginning of the experiment  

> 212

were excluded from the analysis (S1A Fig).

> 214

We examined whether participants learned effectively under each volatility condition in  

> 215

> 216

> 217

> 219

95% CI = 0.59, 0.69, d = 1.46). In the M2 condition, the better choice was defined as a selection  

> 220

more likely to lead to a state with either the highest reward or a higher mean reward for that trial.  

> 221

The better choice rate significantly surpassed the chance level for both the highest reward ( t(17) = 

> 222

5.97, p = 1.5e −5, 95% CI = 0.58, 0.68, d = 1.41) and higher mean reward conditions ( t(17) = 5.41,  

> 223

p = 4.7e −5, 95% CI = 0.57, 0.66, d = 1.28). However, there was no significant difference between  

> 224

conditions under both definitions (highest: F(3, 51) = 1.49, p = 0.23, η2 = 0.044; higher mean: F(3,  

> 225

51) = 2.37, p = 0.08, η2 = 0.067). These results indicated that participants in the Aware group  

> 226

learned effectively across all conditions.  

> 227

We conducted two primary analyses: model-agnostic and model-fitting analysis. The  

> 228

former included one-trial [8] and multitrial back analyses [41]. Learning effects can be assessed  

> 229

without relying on specific computational models by examining how past trial experiences  

> 230

influence subsequent choices. Trial experiences were characterized by the transition  

> 231

(common/rare) and reward. Contributions from the most recent trial and multiple preceding trials  

> 232

were assessed using one-trial and multitrial back analyses (Fig 3). Learning under the MF-RL  

> 233

strategy was reflected in the main effect of reward, whereas learning under the MB-RL strategy  

> 234

was captured by the transition–reward interaction. Consistent with this account, the RL simulation  

> 235

exhibited that greater weighting of the MB-RL strategy was associated with a larger transition ×  

> 236

reward interaction and smaller main effects of reward (Fig 3). Notably, differences in reward  

> 237

volatility led to variations in effect sizes, even when the underlying learning strategies remain  

> 238

unchanged (S3A Fig). This issue was addressed in the one-trial back analysis by including a  

> 239

predictor indicating whether the better choice was selected on the immediately preceding trial (S3A  

> 240

Fig; see Methods) [37, 42] and in the multitrial back analysis by including predictors of multiple  

> 241

preceding trials [41, 42].  

> 242

Model-fitting analyses were performed by fitting a hybrid RL model to the data [8]. In 12  

> 243

model-agnostic analyses, parameters other than the MF–MB balance can also influence the  

> 244

interaction between transition and reward, as well as the main effects of reward (S4A Fig).  

> 245

Consequently, differences in effect sizes across reward volatility conditions could not be uniquely  

> 246

attributed to changes in the MF–MB balance. To address this issue, we assumed that all parameters  

> 247

could vary across conditions and estimated them independently for each condition. This approach  

> 248

prevented changes in other parameters from being confounded with changes in the balance  

> 249

parameter across conditions, although this model was not best fitted (S5 Fig). We confirmed that  

> 250

the model permits reliable parameter recovery without introducing unjustified bias across  

> 251

conditions, at least for the balance parameter (S6 Fig).

# High time pressure prompts a longer history-dependent MF-RL strategy  

> 317

We examined whether participants learned effectively for each ITI condition in the Aware  

> 318

# Benefit simulation  

> 349

The effect of reward volatility on the MF–MB balance might be explained by differences  

> 350

in expected reward [20, 38]. We simulated this type of benefit under our task settings using a  

> 351

similar method as Kool et al. [20]. Specifically, the mean reward for the MF-RL and MB-RL  

> 352

strategies was simulated across various levels of volatility and a range of learning rates and inverse  

> 353

temperatures (Fig 2D; S11 Fig). The benefit was defined as the difference in the volumes under  

> 354

the mean reward surfaces between the MB-RL and MF-RL strategies as a function of learning  

> 355

rates and inverse temperatures. The benefit was smaller in L1 than in M1 and H1, whereas the  

> 356

difference between M1 and H1 was small. The results were similar when we simulated the MB–  

> 357

MF difference in expected reward using reward sequences allocated for each participant and the  

> 358

parameters estimated by the hybrid model (S12 Fig). 17

# Systematic examination of the effect of reward volatility on learning  

> 360

# strategy (Experiment 2)  

> 361

In Experiment 1, higher reward volatility promoted MB control, consistent with previous  

> 362

findings [38]. However, because the simulated benefit remained large even under the highest  

> 363

volatility (Fig 2D; S11 Fig; S12 Fig), it remains unclear which learning strategy dominates when  

> 364

reward volatility is sufficiently high that neither MF-RL nor MB-RL arbitration changes when  

> 365

reward contingencies fluctuate so rapidly that MB control loses its adaptive advantage. To address  

> 366

this question, we systematically examined the effect of reward volatility on learning strategies by  

> 367

introducing four volatility conditions, including extremely high and low levels of volatility that  

> 368

yield relatively small benefits. We conducted a power analysis based on the one-trial back analysis  

> 369

using a generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) [43] and preregistered a criterion to recruit 25  

> 370

participants for the Aware group (https://osf.io/47uv9).  

> 371

# Task structure  

> 372

We required 53 participants to perform a modified version of the two-step task (Fig 7A)  

> 373

[8]. The number of actions available for each second-step state was always one, and the ITI was  

> 374

fixed at 350 ms. The reward was given probabilistically. The reward probabilities for the blue and  

> 375

orange states were defined by four distinct joint probability distributions (Fig 7B). Rewards were  

> 376

generated from one of these distributions for several consecutive trials, after which the reward  

> 377

distribution switched randomly to one of the other distributions. This reward structure requires  

> 378

participants to learn the values of the blue and orange states independently [42], unlike in reversal  

> 379

tasks, and permits large, discrete changes in reward probability even under low-volatility  

> 380

conditions, unlike Gaussian random walk procedures. The Poisson distribution controlled the  

> 381

number of trials until the next switch in the reward distribution (Fig 7C). Reward volatility was 18  

> 382

defined as the Poisson distribution parameter λ. We used four conditions corresponding to λ of 2,  

> 383

5, 23, and 52. These values were selected according to the benefit simulation (Fig 7D; S11 Fig).  

> 384

Importantly, the benefit was reduced when volatility was either excessively high or low compared  

> 385

with a moderate level. To align with the convention that larger values indicate higher volatility, we  

> 386

reported the results using the negative logarithm of λ as the measure of reward volatility. Note that  

> 387

this transformation did not affect the analysis, as the reward volatility was treated as a categorical  

> 388

variable.  

> 389

Although reward volatility was operationalized differently across the two experiments,  

> 390

both manipulations were designed to control changes in reward contingencies over trials. In  

> 391

Experiment 1, volatility was defined as the SD of a Gaussian random walk governing reward  

> 392

magnitudes, whereas in Experiment 2, volatility was defined by the rate of switches between  

> 393

reward-probability distributions. Thus, both experiments assessed how changes in reward  

> 394

contingencies modulate MF–MB arbitration, whereas Experiment 2 extended the range of  

> 395

volatility to include conditions in which the adaptive advantage of MB control was expected to  

> 396

diminish.  

> 397

As in Experiment 1, each volatility condition comprised 60 trials, and participants  

> 398

completed each condition twice (Table 1). We designed four block sequences to offset sequence  

> 399

effects, with each participant assigned to one. After the task instructions, participants completed  

> 400

20 practice trials using different stimulus sets from the main trials and performed 480 main trials.  

> 401

Lastly, participants answered questions, including verbal reports of the state-transition probabilities.

> 495

Because changes in various parameters can influence effect sizes in model-agnostic  

> 496

analysis (S4 Fig), differences in effect sizes across conditions do not necessarily reflect changes in 23  

> 497

the balance between the MF-RL and MB-RL strategies. Accordingly, we performed an analysis  

> 498

that associated the one-trial back analysis with the RL simulation to dissociate changes in the  

> 499

balance parameter from changes in the learning rate and inverse temperature (S13 Fig; see  

> 500

Methods). To investigate the relationship between parameter changes and their corresponding  

> 501

effect sizes in the one-trial back analysis, we constructed a map from the space of parameter  

> 502

changes to that of effect sizes using RL simulation. These simulations demonstrated that changes  

> 503

in the balance parameter can be distinguished from changes in either the learning rate or inverse  

> 504

temperature (S13AB Fig), although changes in the learning rate and inverse temperature could not  

> 505

be dissociated (S13C Fig). The estimated effect size from experimental data was plotted in the  

> 506

effect size space. Using the map constructed by the RL simulation, we inferred the position of the  

> 507

effect size in the space of parameter changes. The difference in effect sizes between the −log(2)  

> 508

and −log(5) conditions was explained by increases in the balance parameter and the learning rate  

> 509

(or inverse temperature; S13AB Fig). The difference in effect sizes between the −log(2) and  

> 510

−log(23) conditions and that between the −log(2) and −log(52) conditions were explained by  

> 511

decreases in the balance parameter and increases in the learning rate (or inverse temperature;  

> 512

S13AB Fig). Furthermore, these differences were not explained solely by changes in the learning  

> 513

rate and inverse temperature (S13C Fig).

# Discussion  

> 547

Animals must adjust their learning strategies in response to their current environmental  

> 548

conditions. We examined the effect of reward volatility on the arbitration between MF-RL and  

> 549

MB-RL strategies. Across both experiments, we observed a consistent nonlinear relationship  

> 550

between reward volatility and the balance between the MF-RL and MB-RL strategies: MB-RL  

> 551

was most strongly driven under moderate reward volatility, provided that the invariant structure of  

> 552

the state transition had been learned. Additionally, high time pressure promoted the use of a longer  

> 553

history-dependent MF learning strategy.  

> 554

# Implication for cost–benefit optimization  

> 555

The results of the experiments and RL simulations suggest that the nonlinear relationship  

> 556

between reward volatility and the MF–MB balance reflects cost–benefit optimization [20, 21]. The  

> 557

time pressure effect indicates that the MB-RL strategy incurs higher time costs than the MF-RL  

> 558

strategy. The MB-RL strategy was most strongly engaged under intermediate reward volatility,  

> 559

under which the benefit was higher than the highest or lowest volatility. Nevertheless, the  

> 560

condition under which the benefit was maximal in the RL simulations did not correspond precisely  

> 561

to the condition in which the MB-RL strategy was most strongly engaged experimentally. Several  

> 562

explanations could explain this discrepancy. First, although the benefit simulations were  

> 563

conducted independently for each volatility condition, participants are likely to adapt their learning  

> 564

strategies as they estimate volatility online. To examine whether online volatility estimation shifts  

> 565

the peak of the benefit, it is necessary to implement a volatility estimation algorithm, as described 26  

> 566

later in the text. Second, there might be individual differences in the cost of engaging the MB-RL  

> 567

strategy such that individuals with lower MB costs might use the MB-RL strategy even under  

> 568

higher levels of reward volatility. Third, the estimated benefit might be influenced by prior beliefs,  

> 569

akin to Bayesian integration. Because the relationship between reward volatility and benefit  

> 570

depends on task structure, priors shaped by everyday experience might cause participants’ benefit  

> 571

estimates to deviate from the task-specific benefit computed in the simulations. Alternatively,  

> 572

instead of explicitly computing benefits, participants might utilize heuristics that adjust the balance  

> 573

between MF-RL and MB-RL strategies based directly on reward volatility.  

> 574

Kool et al. conducted a pioneering study that demonstrated the arbitration between MF  

> 575

and MB based on the benefits [21]. Our study supports their theory and adds new findings. Kool  

> 576

et al. found that humans employed a more MB-RL strategy in a high-benefit task than in a low- 

> 577

benefit task [21]. However, as they simultaneously manipulated five factors, including the reward  

> 578

volatility, to change the benefit, it remained unclear which factors drove the MB-RL strategy. Our  

> 579

study demonstrated that the reward volatility influenced the balance between MF and MB.  

> 580

Additionally, unlike the task used by Kool et al. [21], our task included only one state in the first  

> 581

step, enabling us to eliminate the possibility of functional generalization in the MF-RL strategy.  

> 582

Previous studies reported that value generalizes across perceptually distinct stimuli that share the  

> 583

same outcomes [45, 46]. Although it remains unclear whether such value sharing arises from the  

> 584

MF-RL strategy with functional generalization or the MB-RL strategy, it is difficult to distinguish  

> 585

between the two factors in certain task structures. In the task used by Kool et al. , the first step  

> 586

consisted of two states, each of which included an action that deterministically transitioned to the  

> 587

same second-step state [21]. Although this task structure increases the benefit, it does not eliminate  

> 588

the possibility that participants used an MF-RL strategy with functional generalization.  

> 589

Conversely, in our task, first-step actions have opponent transition structures, allowing us to  

> 590

exclude this possibility. Consequently, our results can be interpreted as reflecting the balance 27  

> 591

between MF-RL and MB-RL strategies rather than the degree of functional generalization.  

> 592

# Learning strategies under dynamic and uncertain environments  

> 593

The learning strategy differed substantially between the Aware and Unaware groups. Both  

> 594

model-agnostic and model-fitting analyses demonstrated that the Aware group was strongly biased  

> 595

toward the MB-RL strategy, whereas the Unaware group was strongly biased toward the MF-RL  

> 596

strategy. These results indicate that explicit awareness of state-transition probabilities is critical to  

> 597

the use of the MB-RL strategy. This is consistent with previous findings that humans primarily use  

> 598

the MB-RL strategy when instructed about the state-transition structure using the cover story [47,  

> 599

48], and the learning strategy shifts from MF-RL to MB-RL following instructions about the task  

> 600

structure [49].  

> 601

Notably, our results suggest that the MF–MB balance is influenced by reward volatility,  

> 602

even if an explicit model of the state transition is sufficiently constructed to report it verbally. A  

> 603

nonlinear relationship between reward volatility and the balance parameter was observed only in  

> 604

the Aware group, in which participants could report the state-transition probability correctly by at  

> 605

least the end of the task. Our findings captured changes in learning strategies under uncertain state- 

> 606

transition models, rather than being inconsistent with those reported by Feher da Silva and Hare  

> 607

and Castro-Rodrigues et al. [47, 49]. In laboratory settings detached from daily life, explicit  

> 608

instructions from the experimenter might substantially reduce uncertainty in participants’ models  

> 609

of task–state-transition probabilities. Conversely, under uncertain models without explicit  

> 610

instructions, the balance between MF-RL and MB-RL strategies might vary with environmental  

> 611

conditions.  

> 612

# Effect of time pressure on the learning strategies 28  

> 613

We found that a longer history-dependent MF-RL strategy dominated decision-making  

> 614

under high time pressure. As this result was observed in the Aware group, high time pressure  

> 615

disrupted learning based on an established model of state transitions rather than its formation. This  

> 616

result indicates that the MB-RL strategy requires more computational processes than the MF-RL  

> 617

strategy, consistent with the findings of several studies highlighting the high cost of the MB-RL  

> 618

strategy [35, 50, 51]. Notably, across all of these studies, participants adaptively shifted to the low- 

> 619

cost MF-RL strategy rather than stopping learning. This means that humans can switch between  

> 620

low-cost and high-cost learning strategies depending on the environmental conditions.  

> 621

Additionally, we revealed that high time pressure decreases the learning rate and eligibility trace,  

> 622

resulting in a longer history-dependent learning strategy. This was adaptive in Experiment 1  

> 623

because the reward was given as a magnitude rather than a probability ( e.g. , Fig 2D). Indeed,  

> 624

participants’ performance was higher under low time pressure than under high time pressure (S2  

> 625

Fig). The reduced influence of newly acquired information on decision-making under high time  

> 626

pressure might underlie the increased reliance on heuristic judgments in time-pressured contexts  

> 627

[52].  

> 628

# Effect of the complexity of the state–action space on the learning  

> 629

# strategies  

> 630

We found that the complex state space promoted the perseverative MF-RL strategy (S8  

> 631

Fig). The effect of the complexity of the state space on the MF–MB balance is consistent with the  

> 632

findings of Kim et al. , who noted that humans tended to increasingly use the MB-RL strategy in  

> 633

response to high complexity when the uncertainty of the state transition was low (90% vs. 10%)  

> 634

whereas they resorted to the MF-RL strategy under high uncertainty (50% vs. 50%) and  

> 635

complexity [53]. Moreover, a complex state–action space promoted perseverative learning  

> 636

strategies. Lai and Gershman suggested that perseveration naturally emerges as a resolution of the 29  

> 637

tradeoff between reward maximization and policy complexity [54]. As complex policies occupy  

> 638

cognitive resources, policy compression increases perseveration to resolve them. The complexity  

> 639

of the state–action space might strain cognitive resources, leading to increased perseverative  

> 640

behavior.  

> 641

# Implication for neural implementation  

> 642

The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) might play an important role in the neural  

> 643

mechanisms underlying arbitration of the MF–MB balance in response to reward volatility. The  

> 644

ACC has been implicated in the encoding of reward volatility [27]. It also plays a critical role in  

> 645

the MB-RL strategy. For example, a meta-analysis of fMRI experiments using a two-step task  

> 646

found that the ACC is important for the MB-RL strategy [55]. In addition, the monkey’s ACC  

> 647

and striatum neurons encoded action value for the MB-RL strategy [56], and optogenetic inhibition  

> 648

of the rat’s ACC disrupted the MB-RL strategy [57]. Moreover, the ACC encodes multiple  

> 649

environmental statistics, evaluates the cost–benefit of various strategies based on these statistics,  

> 650

and determines the system and its strength to control behavior [58, 59]. However, it remains  

> 651

unknown whether the ACC monitors the environmental statistics or controls the behavioral  

> 652

strategy [60]. Importantly, our task can dissociate the neural encoding of reward volatility from the  

> 653

arbitration between MF-RL and MB-RL strategies because of the nonlinear relationship between  

> 654

them: if one region encodes reward volatility, then its activity is expected to be modulated linearly  

> 655

by reward volatility, whereas if it regulates the balance between the MF-RL and MB-RL strategies,  

> 656

its activity is expected to be modulated nonlinearly by reward volatility.  

> 657

# Candidate computational algorithm for tracking reward volatility  

> 658

Previous studies proposed several algorithms to detect reward volatility. Behrens et al.  

> 659

proposed a hierarchical Bayesian learner to estimate reward volatility [27]. Several other previous 30  

> 660

studies proposed approximate methods with lower computational costs. For example, approaches  

> 661

based on variational Bayes [61], particle filters [23], and particle filters with a noise scaled by the  

> 662

distance between prior and posterior distributions have been proposed [62, 63]. Wang et al.  

> 663

suggested that a recurrent neural network in the prefrontal cortex develops a learning system that  

> 664

supports meta-learning through RL by the dopamine system [64]. This network can adapt its  

> 665

learning rate based on reward volatility and produce the MB-RL strategy for the two-step task. By  

> 666

incorporating cost-dependent adjustments of learning strategies into these algorithms, the results  

> 667

observed in the present study might be reproduced.  

> 668

# Limitations  

> 669

One limitation of our study was that it reduced the diversity of learning strategies to a single  

> 670

dimension across the MF-RL and MB-RL strategies [47, 65]. Estimating the MF–MB balance  

> 671

using hybrid models can lead to misclassification; thus, MF-RL strategies might be mistakenly  

> 672

interpreted as MB-RL strategies, and vice versa. The MF-RL strategy with state representations  

> 673

based on specific combinations of the experienced state transition and reward on the last trial, as  

> 674

well as the MF-RL strategy with inferred latent states related to reward probabilities, can mimic  

> 675

MB-like behavior [42]. These strategies are unlikely to account for participants’ behavior in our  

> 676

task for multiple reasons. First, the results of the multitrial back analysis indicated that participants’  

> 677

behavior was influenced by multiple past trials, suggesting they were not relying on state  

> 678

representations based solely on the most recent trial. Second, as the reward function in our task  

> 679

was independent across second-step states, constructing latent states was more difficult than in  

> 680

reversal-learning structures, in which the values of both second-step states can be inferred from a  

> 681

single choice. In addition, it has been demonstrated that fitting the hybrid RL model to data that  

> 682

include learning or behavioral tendencies not assumed by the hybrid model can bias the estimates  

> 683

of the balance parameter toward the MF component, which has greater flexibility because of its 31  

> 684

larger number of free parameters [47]. Consequently, even if participants rely on the pure MB-RL  

> 685

strategy across all volatility conditions, the hybrid model might infer a shift toward the MF-RL  

> 686

strategy when systematic deviations from the model assumptions are particularly salient in specific  

> 687

volatility conditions. However, this possibility is also unlikely to explain our results. First, not only  

> 688

model-fitting analyses model-agnostic one-trial and multitrial back analyses revealed an increased  

> 689

interaction effect between state transitions and rewards in the intermediate volatility condition.  

> 690

Second, the goodness-of-fit, quantified by the log-likelihood, was not correlated with the balance  

> 691

parameter (S16 Fig), contradicting the findings of Feher da Silva and Hare [47]. This lack of  

> 692

correlation suggests that tendencies not captured by the hybrid model did not bias parameter  

> 693

estimates toward the MF component.  

> 694

Additionally, a successor representation (SR), which learns values from the cumulative  

> 695

future-state occupancy of each state–action pair, can exhibit MB-like behavior in the two-step task  

> 696

[66]. As our task does not allow us to dissociate SR from MB-RL strategies, it remains possible  

> 697

that participants employed an SR-based strategy. To distinguish among these strategies, a  

> 698

behavioral task that distinguishes among MF, SR, and MB strategies is required [67].  

> 699

Taken together, our results revealed a nonlinear relationship between reward volatility and  

> 700

the balance between low-cost MF-RL and high-cost MB-RL strategies only among participants  

> 701

who were aware of the biased state-transition structure. These results support the cost–benefit  

> 702

arbitration between MF-RL and MB-RL strategies in dynamic and uncertain environments.

> 725

> 726

> 727

> 736

> 746

> 747

> 903

> 905

> 949

The objective of RL is to learn the probabilities of choosing actions that maximize rewards.  

> 950

> 951

> 952

> 953

> 954

# Parameter recovery  

> 1011

To confirm the reliability of parameter estimation without introducing unjustified bias  

> 1012

across conditions, we conducted a parameter recovery analysis using a model in which all  

> 1013

parameters were estimated independently (S6 Fig). For both experiments, we generated 160  

> 1014

parameter sets and simulated data based on them. The learning rate, eligibility trace, and balance  

> 1015

parameters were drawn from beta distributions (1, 1); inverse temperature was generated using  

> 1016

gamma distribution (2, 4); and perseveration was generated using a normal distribution (0, 3) in  

> 1017

Experiment 1 and (0, 0.3) in Experiment 2, respectively. For Experiment 1, the values sampled  

> 1018

from the Gamma distribution were divided by 10 before being used as inverse-temperature  

> 1019

parameter values. Parameter values were fixed across conditions.
