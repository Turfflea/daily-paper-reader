Abstract 11 Semantic segmentation of domain-specific imaging data where labelled training examples are 12 scarce and foreground–background contrast is low remains an open challenge in deep 13 learning applied to science. Palaeontological computed tomography (CT) exemplifies this 14 problem: digitally isolating fossilised bone from surrounding rock matrix is labour-intensive 15 (≥100 hrs/dataset), subjective, and often reliant on expensive proprietary software, creating a 16 “segmentation bottleneck” that prevents large-scale and rapid processing of CT data 17 collections. Here we present a self-supervised, end-to-end framework combining SimCLR v1 18 contrastive pretraining with deterministic pseudo-label generation and U-Net refinement to 19 fully automate fossil CT segmentation without manual annotation. Using 50,626 CT images 20 from the Middle Jurassic Kilmaluag Formation spanning amphibians, reptiles, dinosaurs, and 21 early mammals, the framework achieved a Dice coefficient of 93.66% and IoU of 82.42% on 22 a held-out specimen not seen during training, comparable to the highest Dice and IoU values 23 reported in recent Deep Learning-based fossil CT segmentation studies. Cross-taxon 24 generalisation was validated geometrically on six fully external specimens, achieving sub-25 voxel mesh agreement with manually thresholded references. By eliminating the annotation 26 requirement that has limited prior deep learning approaches in palaeontology, this framework 27 reduces per-specimen processing from ~100 person-hours to 6 hrs (one-time UNet training) 28 +1-3 minutes (mesh generation per specimen), an essential first step towards batch processing 29 and analysis of CT data for large-scale comparative and quantitative analyses. 30

This has created a profound ‘segmentation bottleneck,’ 53 whereby CT data are generated faster than they can be processed within realistic academic 54 timeframes, severely constraining large-scale palaeontological research and limiting the 55 scientific potential of existing CT collections. Furthermore, the time cost that is incurred 56 during manual segmentation could be better budgeted to downstream anatomical, 57 phylogenetic, and functional analyses. This challenge is also an open problem in machine 58 learning (ML) and deep learning (DL): fossil CT data presents extreme foreground– 59 background contrast ambiguity, severe class imbalance due to incomplete preservation, and 60 morphological diversity that exceeds the assumptions of standard segmentation architectures 61 trained on biomedical or natural photographic datasets (e.g., ImageNet). 62 A foundational technique in virtual palaeontology is global thresholding 2. Also 63 referred to as iso-surfacing, this method assumes that fossilised material and the enclosing 64 matrix occupy distinct, non-overlapping ranges of radiodensity in images, expressed as 65 attenuation coefficients 10 . In practice, however, the bimodal histogram required for clean 66 thresholding is the exception. The result is 72 segmentation errors including matrix bleed, loss of thin structures such as turbinates or 73 trabeculae, and artificial fusion of adjacent skeletal elements 11-13 . As a result, researchers 74 were forced to adopt local adaptive thresholding (or manual painting approaches). 75 In response, the software industry introduced semi-automated segmentation solutions. 76 Commercial platforms such as Avizo 14 , Mimics 15 , Volume Graphics StudioMAX 16 , and 77 Dragonfly 17 became standard tools in digital palaeontology. These packages implemented 78 algorithms including region growing/shrinking 18 , watershed segmentation 19 , and slice 79 interpolation 20 , allowing users to define seed regions or segment every n th slice while the 80 software interpolated intervening volumes. More advanced tools, such as Dragonfly’s 81 Segmentation Wizard 21 and the Trainable Weka Segmentation 22 plugin in ImageJ, employ 82 classical Machine Learning classifiers, most commonly Random Forests 23 or Support Vector 83 Machines 24 , operating on local texture features. In these workflows, the user manually 84 annotates a subset of voxels to define classes such as bone, matrix, or air, and the classifier 85 predicts labels for the remainder of the volume based on voxel intensity and local texture 86 statistics. Although these approaches represented a substantive advance over fully manual 87 segmentation, several limitations prevent effective scaling 6,8 . First, they require a persistent 88 human-in-the-loop and remain fundamentally interactive rather than automated. Domain 89 experts must generate training data for each specimen, rendering these workflows impractical 90 for large-scale segmentation of CT datasets. Second, the resulting classifiers are typically 91 specimen-specific, overfitted to the contrast, noise characteristics, and morphology of 92 individual scans, and rarely transferable without retraining. As a result, training effort is 93 effectively discarded after each session, leading to extensive duplication of labour across the 94 field. Third, reliance on proprietary software undermines reproducibility and accessibility. 95 Segmentation workflows implemented in commercial platforms often function as black 96 boxes, with algorithmic details, feature selection, and parameterisation obscured. While 97 Dragonfly offers a FreeD licence for qualifying academic users, this programme excludes 98 government institutions, non-profit organisations, and researchers in several major research 99 territories including Japan, South Korea, Singapore, and Mainland China including Hong 100 Kong and Macau 25,26 . In addition, reports of early accuracy 147 plateaux following minimal annotation or training effort suggest that some models may 148 overfit to narrowly defined datasets and tolerate relatively high false-positive or false-149 negative rates when applied beyond the original specimens or scanning conditions 6,9,31 .150 To address these persistent challenges, we introduce a self-supervised, end-to-end 151 segmentation framework that effectively bypasses the need for initial manual annotation. Our 152 framework utilises SimCLR v1 (Simple Framework for Contrastive Learning of Visual 153 Representations ver.1) 34 as a pre-training phase, where it learns high-level feature 154 representations directly from raw, unlabelled CT slices through stochastic augmentations. 155 Concomitantly, we implemented a suite of rule-based, deterministic image processing 156 algorithms to generate a standardised preliminary set of ground truth masks. Unlike traditional workflows that discard training effort after 162 each specimen, this framework was implemented on a dataset of 50,626 CT image slices. 163 This dataset, derived from vertebrate fossils comprising amphibians, reptiles, dinosaurs and 164 early mammals, represents a five-fold increase over the largest training set previously 165 employed in palaeontological CT segmentation (7,986 slices) 6 , and is orders of magnitude 166 larger than the 5-18 manually annotated slices used in most recent fossil CT segmentation 167 studies 7,9,30 .168 The unprecedented scale and taxonomic breadth of this dataset are critical for 169 establishing a reproducible alternative to human-in-the-loop segmentation. Results 185 

Contrastive pre-training encodes fossil-matrix structure without labels 186 The SimCLR v1 self-supervised pre-training, conducted on 39,037 unlabelled CT slices 187 drawn from a taxonomically diverse corpus of 50,626 images yielded robust, domain-specific 188 feature representations that reliably discriminate fossilised bone from surrounding geological 189 matrix without requiring manual annotation. Over 250 training epochs, contrastive accuracy 190 converged to 93.89% on the training set and 93.66% on the held-out validation set ( Fig. 2b ), 191 with corresponding normalised temperature cross entropy (NT-Xent) losses of 0.2036 and 192 0.2312 ( Fig. 2a ). The narrow gap between training and validation metrics indicates effective 193 generalisation rather than memorisation. Both loss curves descend steeply during the first 194 approximately 50 epochs before plateauing ( Fig. 2a ), while accuracy curves rise in parallel 195 with no divergence ( Fig. 2b ), confirming stable convergence without overfitting. 196 To verify that the learned representations encode biologically meaningful structure 197 rather than radiographic noise, we applied two complementary post-hoc validation 198 techniques. Uniform Manifold Approximation Project (UMAP) dimensionality reduction 36 199 was applied to the 2048-dimensional feature vectors extracted by the trained ResNet-50 200 encoder 37 , projecting them into two-dimensional space ( Fig. 2c ). The projection is overlaid 201 with a Gaussian Kernel Density Estimate (KDE), mapping point density from dark purple 202 (sparse) through teal and green (moderate) to yellow (highest-density cores). Crucially, no 203 class labels are used; all visible structure has emerged entirely from the contrastive objective. 204 This emergent clustering is characteristic of well-trained contrastive encoders 34,38 and serves 205 as a standard quality check for self-supervised representations. 206

Rule-based masking generates reproducible bone-location priors at scale 227 A critical step enabling end-to-end automation is the parallel generation of standardised 228 ground truth masks through a fully deterministic, rule-based image processing pipeline. 229 Rather than relying on human annotators to manually delineate bone in each CT slice, we 230 developed a sequence of classical image processing operations with fixed parameters, applied 231 identically across the entire test partition (3 datasets, 7,824 images) to produce coarse but 232 consistent binary masks of fossilised bone ( Fig. 3c , middle column). These masks served not 233 as final segmentation outputs, but as reproducible spatial guidance for the subsequent U-Net 234 training through the knowledge fusion strategy ( Fig. 1 ). 235 The output is illustrated in Fig. 3c , where three representative CT slices spanning 236 different specimens and anatomical configurations are shown alongside their corresponding 237 automated masks. The left column presents the original CT images, revealing the 238 characteristic challenge of the Kilmaluag Formation material: fossilised bone and dolomitic 239 matrix share similar grey-level intensities. The middle column presents the automated ground 240 truth masks. These masks capture the gross morphology of the skeletal material in each slice, 241 delineating the major bone cross-sections and preserving the overall spatial arrangement of 242 anatomical elements. Knowledge fusion yields high-precision 3D bone reconstructions 262 The knowledge fusion stage combined the two independent information streams, domain-263 specific feature representations from SimCLR v1 pre-training and standardised spatial 264 guidance from the rule-based automated masks, into a single segmentation model. A modified 265 U-Net architecture 41 initialised with SimCLR-trained ResNet-50 encoder weights refined the 266 coarse mask-image pairs into high-precision segmentation overlays. Using a composite 267 Weighted Cross-Entropy and Dice loss function 42 to address the pronounced class imbalance 268 between bone and matrix voxels, training and test losses converged within 50 epochs and 269 plateaued without divergence (Fig. 3a). On the held-out test dataset, the model achieved a 270 Dice coefficient of 0.9366 and IoU of 0.8242 at the best checkpoint (Fig. 3b), confirming 271 generalisation beyond the training data. 272 The qualitative improvement over the coarse automated masks is directly visible in 273 Fig. 3c. Where relatively well-defined bone cross-sections are present (upper two rows), the 274 U-Net predicted overlays closely follow anatomical boundaries with visibly sharper edges, 275 respecting fine morphological detail including narrow bone processes and thin cortical 276 margins that the rule-based algorithm captured only approximately. Our AI-framework generated meshes achieve sub-voxel agreement with manual 306 

thresholding 307 To assess the fidelity of the AI-generated meshes against an established palaeontological 308 workflow, the six specimens segmented by the SimCLR+U-Net pipeline were independently 309 segmented using binary thresholding in Avizo at multiple threshold levels ( Supplementary 310 

Information , Fig. S1 ). For each specimen, both meshes were scaled to a common 1:1 311 coordinate space using their respective voxel sizes prior to registration, ensuring dimensional 312 equivalence without relying on algorithmic scale adjustment. The resulting mesh pairs were 313 then compared through a three-phase geometric registration protocol in CloudCompare v2. 314 First, Point Pair Registration (PPR) 43 established a common coordinate system 315 through manual landmarking, yielding coarse alignments with RMS errors ranging from 316 0.081 mm to 0.363 mm across the assemblage. Second, Iterative Closest Point (ICP) 317 refinement 44 minimised nearest-neighbour distances through rigid transformation (rotation 318 and translation only), with scale fixed at 1.0 throughout to confirm that the pre-registered 319 meshes are dimensionally equivalent and to prevent the algorithm from artificially warping 320 either mesh toward noise clusters. Third, Cloud-to-Cloud (C2C) 45 signed distance analysis 321 quantified per-point deviations between the full automated point cloud and the manual 322 reference surface, yielding mean distance, standard deviation, and Hausdorff (maximum) 323 distance statistics. 324 For Salamander A (Specimen 1), Salamander A (Specimen 2) and Docodonta 325 (undescribed), ICP overlap was set to 100%, meaning all points were used and the resulting 326 RMS captures full geometric divergence. For Mammaliaformes indet., Marmorerpeton wakei 327 (Specimens 1 and 2), overlap was set to 80%, computing correspondences on only the best-328 matching 80% of points and discarding the worst 20% as outliers during each iteration. This 329 trimmed setting was applied to specimens exhibiting significant non-overlapping geometry: 330 multi-element vertebral complexes and fragmented specimens with prominent ring artefacts, 331 where outlier fragments would otherwise bias the rigid alignment of core anatomy. The C2C 332 signed distances in the third phase were computed on the full, untrimmed point clouds for all 333 six specimens regardless of the ICP overlap setting ( Fig. 4 ). 334

Discussion 386 This study presents a fully automated, self-supervised segmentation framework that removes 387 the need for human annotation in palaeontological CT workflows and achieves sub-voxel 388 geometric agreement with established manual methods for well-preserved specimens. For 389 specimens affected by ring artefacts or fragmentation, the pipeline correctly excludes 390 reconstruction noise that manual thresholding retains. The SimCLR+U-Net pipeline, trained 391 on 39,037 unlabelled CT slices spanning amphibians, reptiles, dinosaurs, and early mammals, 392 attained a Dice coefficient of 0.9366 and IoU of 0.8242 on a held-out test specimen not seen 393 during training, comparable to the highest Dice and IoU values reported in recent DL-based 394 fossil CT segmentation studies 6,7,9,30-32,46 . External geometric comparison against binary 395 thresholding in Avizo yielded C2C means of 0.63 to 0.75 voxels for the best-performing 396 specimens at a fixed scale of 1.0. 397 The adoption of SimCLR v1 as a self-supervised pre-training strategy addresses the 398 most persistent obstacle to applying deep learning in palaeontological CT: the need for large 399 volumes of expert-annotated training labels. In standard workflows, specialists manually 400 trace bone boundaries on each training slice, and this effort is discarded after each specimen 401 and repeated from scratch for new material. The contrastive objective used here learns 402 directly from 39,037 unlabelled slices drawn from a corpus of 50,626 images, extracting 403 feature representations that capture fossil textures, taphonomic noise signatures, and beam 404 hardening patterns without any supervisory signal. This corpus substantially exceeds all 405 previously published fossil CT training sets in both scale and taxonomic breadth. The 406 representations learned on palaeontological material are expected to outperform generic 407 ImageNet-pretrained weights 47 for fossil-matrix discrimination, because ImageNet features 408 are optimised for natural-image statistics (colour, texture, object shape) that have little in 409 common with the greyscale, low-contrast characteristics of μCT data. SimCLR pre-training on unlabelled fossil CT data 587 Palaeontological CT presents specific challenges for deep learning: sparsely labelled data, 588 low signal-to-noise ratio, and radiographic artefacts including beam hardening, ring artefacts, 589 and salt-and-pepper noise. Beyond these, the central technical obstacle is taphonomic density 590 convergence, in which the mineral replacement of biological tissues during fossilisation 591 produces X-ray attenuation coefficients in bone that are nearly indistinguishable from those 592 of the surrounding rock matrix. This convergence is not noise that can be filtered out; it is a 593 structural property of the data that pixel-intensity features alone cannot resolve. The SimCLR 594 v1 framework was adopted specifically to address this ( Fig. 2 ): by training a ResNet-50 595 encoder to extract textural and structural signatures rather than intensity statistics, the model 596 learns to discriminate fossil from matrix in exactly the regime where thresholding fails. 597 Contrastive pre-training is performed on a large corpus of unlabelled images before fine 598 tuning for pixel-wise classification. 599 To capture high inter subject variability (i.e., intrinsic physical and radiographic 600 differences among fossil specimens resulting from microscale taphonomy, morphological 601 diversity, scan quality and artefacts, structural deformation and fragmentation), and complex 602 preservation states, we implemented a domain specific stochastic data augmentation pipeline. 603 For each CT slice, the framework generates a positive pair of augmented images ( x i and x j), 604 through random geometric and intensity-based operations. These include random resizing 605 /cropping (scale = 0.6–1.0), vertical flipping (p=0.5), horizontal flipping (p=0.2), random 606 rotation (±15°, p = 0.3), Gaussian blur (p=0.3) to learn global anatomical organisation and 607 local bone micro-texture, as well as arbitrary fossil orientation within the matrix. Intensity 608 and contrast jitter (brightness: p=0.8, contrast: p=0.8) enforce invariance to physical 609 constraints of X-ray imaging. Pre-training acts as a regulariser, mapping noisy and clean 610 inputs to proximate latent representations, thereby improving robustness to stochastic 611 graininess and structural distortions typical of high resolution μCT data. Dual-stream knowledge transfer to U-Net 648 In the final segmentation phase, we employed a knowledge transfer strategy 53 that integrated 649 domain-specific feature representations derived from self-supervised SimCLR v1 pre-training 650 with the standardised guidance of automatically generated ground-truth masks. By initialising 651 the U-Net with weights from the SimCLR ResNet-50 encoder, the model began with an 652 advanced representation of fossilised bone textures and structural signatures that 653 differentiated them from surrounding geological noise. These learned feature representations, 654 combined with rule-based automated ground-truth masks, provided consistent and 655 deterministic delineations of bone specimens. This dual transfer learning stream ensures that 656 the U-Net did not rely exclusively on raw pixel intensities, which are often unreliable due to 657 taphonomic density convergence, but instead integrated high-level textural cues with the 658 standardised spatial constraints defined by the automated masking algorithm. 659 The core segmentation architecture consisted of a modified U-Net optimised for high-660 resolution volumetric data. In contrast to a conventional U-Net, this configuration 661 incorporated a deeper encoder pathway (e1–e4) with progressively increasing channel depths 662 (64-2048), enabling the extraction of increasingly abstract morphological features (Fig. 1). 663 The central bottleneck was substantially expanded, incorporating a 7×7 convolutional block 664 with integrated ReLU activation functions to model complex spatial relationships at a 7×7 665 resolution. The decoder pathway (up4–up1) symmetrically mirrored the encoder to 666 reconstruct the segmentation map, while skip connections preserved fine-scale structural 667 detail, including delicate fossil boundaries. During training across 500 epochs, the model 668 employed a composite Dice and Cross-Entropy loss function to address the pronounced class 669 imbalance between bone and matrix. Further optimization was carried out using AdamW 54 ,670 which is widely considered highly efficient for deep neural networks, while the 671 OneCycleLR 51 scheduler was applied to rapidly identify an optimal learning rate during 672 training. Training convergence metrics are shown in Fig. 3a, b. Model performance was 673 finally evaluated on the held-out internal test specimen, which was not used during U-Net 674 training. 675

The target was a root mean-squared 702 (RMS) error within a few multiples of the voxel size, ensuring sufficient proximity for fine-703 scale optimisation. Second, a scale-calibrated ICP 44 was performed to further optimise 704 alignment by iteratively minimising nearest-neighbour distances while allowing for minor 705 global scale differences. A satisfactory fit was defined by a scale factor close to 1.0, 706 indicating high dimensional fidelity, and a mean cloud-to-cloud (C2C) 45 distance approaching 707 zero, reflecting near-identical overall surface geometry. Finally, C2C distance and Hausdorff 708 analyses were conducted to quantify local deviations and extreme discrepancies. A low 709 standard deviation indicated consistently tight surface agreement, whereas a high one-sided 710 Hausdorff distance highlighted localised mismatches (such as small bones or noise artefacts) 711 present in one mesh but absent in the other.
