Abstract 23 

Three-dimensional fluorescence microscopy often exhibits anisotropic resolution because axial 24 

information is poorly sampled and more blurred than lateral information, which complicates 25 

quantitative interpretation of fine 3D structures. Although optical remedies and computational 26 

restoration have been explored, many approaches require demanding system calibration or rely on 27 

accurate PSF models and assumptions that are difficult to satisfy across all samples and modalities. 28 

Here we present DeepIso, a self-supervised isotropy restoration framework that couples supervised 29 

pretraining with an internal-learning inference stage to estimate degradation directly from the 30 

measured volume. Without explicit PSF specification or enforced lateral–axial structural 31 

equivalence, DeepIso recovers axial frequency content and improves the continuity of elongated 32 

structures while retaining fine features, with superior performance over existing computational 33 

approaches in terms of both visual inspection and quantitative metrics. The method is validated on 34 

synthetic benchmarks and experimental datasets, demonstrating isotropy enhancement across 35 

confocal, light-sheet, and 3D structured illumination microscopy, thereby supporting downstream 36 

volumetric analysis including segmentation and tracking. 37

1. Introduction 39 

Fluorescence microscopy is an essential tool in life sciences, providing high-resolution and high-40 

specificity imaging vital for investigating cellular structures 1 , cell interactions 2 , disease 41 

mechanisms 3 , and drug development 4 . Unlike two-dimensional (2D) imaging, which captures a 42 

single plane, three-dimensional (3D) fluorescence imaging reconstructs the specimen in its entirety, 43 

offering more comprehensive volumetric coverage of biological sampless 5–7 . However, 3D 44 

imaging via z-stack acquisition poses inherent challenges. Due to the intrinsic diffraction limit of 45 

optics and larger axial sampling intervals, single image volumes exhibit pronounced anisotropic 46 

resolution – axial information is resolved more poorly than lateral information. This anisotropy 47 

significantly hinders accurate dissection and quantitative analysis of intricate 3D biological 48 

structures. 49 

To enhance biological interpretations, researchers have proposed various strategies aimed 50 

at achieving isotropic or near-isotropic resolution in 3D fluorescence imaging. Hardware-based 51 

strategies have emerged, modifying optical systems to extend axial information through 52 

interference-based approaches 8–11 , multiview acquisitions 12–15 , and axially swept light-sheet 53 

microscopy (ASLM) 16,17 . Although effective, these methods often impose practical constraints, 54 3

such as stringent alignment requirements, increased optical complexity, and the need for precise 55 

calibration 18–21 . Furthermore, the associated reconstructions frequently require computationally 56 

intensive workflows for multiview fusion, hindering widespread adoption 22 .57 

Alternatively, computational image restoration methods 23–31 offer another pathway toward 58 

resolution isotropy. For instance, 3D deconvolution algorithms reassign out-of-focus light, thereby 59 

improving the recovery of axial information. Nonetheless, the intrinsic physical and sampling 60 

constraints still lead to a narrower axial optical transfer function (OTF) compared to its lateral 61 

counterpart, limiting the recovery of high-frequency axial information. To overcome this limitation, 62 

deep learning-based restoration techniques have been developed, effectively extracting structural 63 

features from the data. Existing supervised methods, such as content aware image restoration 64 

(CARE) 23 , rely on accurate estimates of the axial point spread function (PSF) and the associated 65 

sampling model, which can lead to inaccuracies and mismatched reconstructions if estimations are 66 

not precise. By contrast, unsupervised generative adversarial network (GAN) approaches, 67 

including CycleGAN variants 32 and SelfNet 33 frameworks, are typically trained on unpaired lateral 68 

and axial datasets. Their success hinges on the implicit assumption of structural similarity between 69 

lateral and axial views to establish meaningful cross-view mappings. Adversarial training, however, 70 

frequently suffers from optimization instability, making it highly sensitive to hyperparameters and 71 

run-to-run variability, which heightens the chance of producing artifacts or hallucinated textures. 72 

More recently, the SSAI-3D framework 34 reformulates isotropy restoration as semi-blind axial 73 

deblurring by transferring knowledge from a natural-image-pretrained deblurring backbone to 74 

microscopy data through sparse fine-tuning. While this approach shows promise, it does have 75 

limitations, including incomplete axial resolution recovery and potential complications in 76 

reconstructing high-resolution images due to the fundamental degradation model mismatch 77 

between microscopy images and natural images. 78 

Building on this body of prior work, we introduce DeepIso, a self-supervised framework 79 

designed to enhance isotropic resolution in fluorescence microscopy through internal learning. 80 

DeepIso integrates supervised pretraining with a self-adaptive inference stage, enabling the system 81 

to learn degradation characteristics directly from the data without requiring accurate PSF 82 

estimation or assuming structural similarity between lateral and axial views. Unlike existing 83 

methods, DeepIso expands the effective axial-frequency bandwidth and recovers complex axial 84 

morphology with high fidelity, preserving continuity of elongated structures and detailed features. 85 

We validated the performance and reconstruction fidelity of DeepIso on both synthetic benchmarks 86 4

and experimental datasets, demonstrating robust isotropy restoration across multiple microscopy 87 

modalities, including confocal, light-sheet, and 3D structured illumination microscopy. In all cases, 88 

DeepIso achieves superior performance over existing computational methods. DeepIso provides a 89 

practical solution for sample-adaptive isotropy enhancement, with broad potential for downstream 90 

analyses in volumetric visualization, segmentation, tracking, and long-term time-lapse analysis in 91 

diverse biological applications. 92

2. Results 94 

2.1. Framework of DeepIso for isotropic resolution enhancement 95 

To achieve isotropic restoration from inherently anisotropic microscopy data, we developed 96 

DeepIso, a self-supervised two-stage framework designed to bridge the resolution gap between 97 

lateral and axial dimensions ( Fig. 1 ). 98 

In Stage-1 Pretraining ( Fig. 1a ), an IsoNet backbone is trained to learn isotropic 99 

representations from synthetically degraded data. To emulate diverse optical conditions and axial 100 

blurring, we construct a coarse blur kernel library consisting of one-dimensional kernels of multiple 101 

orientations and scales ( Methods ). These kernels are convolved with isotropic lateral slices to 102 

generate pseudo-anisotropic inputs. Before training, a pixel alignment step is applied by 103 

downsampling one spatial axis to match the axial pixel size and subsequently upsampling it back 104 

to the lateral pixel size, ensuring uniform voxel spacing and consistent receptive-field sampling 105 

across all directions. The IsoNet is then optimized by minimizing the reconstruction loss between 106 

the isotropic ground truth and its blurred counterpart, yielding a pretrained model that captures 107 

scale-invariant and orientation-invariant isotropic features. In parallel with IsoNet pretraining, we 108 

introduce an auxiliary encoder–decoder pair (IsoEnc/IsoDec) to explicitly learn a compact latent 109 

representation of the anisotropic observation manifold. Specifically, IsoEnc encodes the pseudo-110 

anisotropic volumes generated by the coarse-kernel collection into a low-dimensional latent space, 111 

and IsoDec is trained to reconstruct the corresponding anisotropic volumes by minimizing a self-112 

reconstruction loss. This bottlenecked latent embedding regularizes the learning of isotropic 113 

features by enforcing cross-domain consistency: the restored isotropic prediction should remain 114 

explainable under the learned anisotropic generative pathway, rather than relying on unconstrained 115 

hallucination. The overall design is motivated by developments in internal learning and self-/zero-116 

shot super-resolution, where supervised pretraining is complemented by test-time or sample-117 

specific adaptation to bridge the gap between synthetic degradations and real acquisitions (e.g., 118 5

ZSSR 35,36 , meta-transfer 37 , and related self-supervised fine-tuning strategies 38 ). Consequently, 119 

Stage-1 establishes a stable, modality-agnostic latent space that can be locked in the following 120 

inference stage to provide a reliable internal constraint during test-time adaptation. 121 

Importantly, unlike many GAN-based isotropic restoration methods, DeepIso does not rely 122 

on the implicit assumption that biological structures should appear similar between lateral and axial 123 

views. In Stage-2 Adapting Inference ( Fig. 1b, Supplementary Fig. 1, Supplementary Fig. 2 ), 124 

the pretrained network is internally adapted to real experimental data exhibiting anisotropic 125 

sampling. During this process, the encoder–decoder pair (IsoEnc/IsoDec) is locked to preserve the 126 

latent-space consistency learned from pretraining, while only the intermediate IsoNet parameters 127 

are fine-tuned to accommodate the domain shift. This internal adaption permits inference on raw 128 

anisotropic volumes without external training data or explicit supervision, and it does so without 129 

invoking the common assumption of structural similarity between lateral and axial views. 130 

Consequently, DeepIso yields isotropic restorations that preserve axial morphology with high 131 

fidelity while suppressing spurious artifacts across imaging views. 132

2.2. Class-leading performance of DeepIso on simulated volumetric data 134 

We first validated the isotropic restoration capability of DeepIso using simulated volumetric data, 135 

where both anisotropic inputs and isotropic ground truths were available for direct quantitative 136 

comparison ( Fig. 2 ). Anisotropic inputs were obtained by blurring the simulated ground truth with 137 

a simulated point spread function (PSF) to mimic the anisotropic diffraction-limited response set 138 

by the objective lens and sampling. The simulation volume comprised a heterogeneous mixture of 139 

primitives, including point emitters, line segments, hollow spheres, and hollow cylinders with 140 

varying sizes, positions, and 3D orientations. Notably, for hollow cylinders, the circular end-cap is 141 

predominantly visible in lateral views, whereas the cylindrical sidewall becomes apparent only in 142 

axial views. This design intentionally breaks lateral–axial structural similarity, emulating 143 

specimens where cross-view appearance differs substantially. After reconstruction, DeepIso 144 

effectively recovered isotropic structures across the entire 3D volume, yielding results that closely 145 

matched the ground-truth references ( Fig. 2a ). Detailed inspection of representative sub-regions 146 

(Fig. 2b–e ) demonstrated that DeepIso markedly improved axial views compared with existing 147 

deep-learning-based restoration models, including CARE 23 , CycleGAN 32 , SelfNet 33 , and 148 SSAI3D 34 . DeepIso preserved membrane continuity (yellow arrows), resolved fine tubular and 149 

vesicular features (blue and green arrows), and minimized artifacts such as stripe-like 150 6

hallucinations that were frequently observed in CycleGAN and SelfNet outputs. The recovered 151 

structures exhibited smooth intensity transitions along the axial direction, confirming the model’s 152 

ability to learn depth-aware isotropic representations rather than planar deconvolution priors. 153 

Quantitative analysis further confirmed the superior reconstruction fidelity provided by 154 

DeepIso ( Fig. 2f ). Among all evaluated methods, DeepIso achieved the highest peak signal-to-155 

noise ratio (PSNR) and structural similarity index (SSIM), alongside the lowest root mean square 156 

error (RMSE). These metrics indicate that DeepIso produces restorations with minimal distortion 157 

and noise amplification while maintaining accurate volumetric geometry. Collectively, the results 158 

on synthetic benchmarks demonstrate that DeepIso generalizes robustly to anisotropic inputs and 159 

achieves class-leading isotropic restoration accuracy across a wide range of 3D structural motifs. 160

2.3. DeepIso performance validation with hardware-based isotropic references 162 

To evaluate the capability of DeepIso to recovering fine axial details in experimental data, we 163 

applied it to volumetric datasets acquired by super-resolution and light-sheet modalities, where we 164 

could obtain near-isotropic ground truth ( Fig. 3 ). In a first example, we used DeepIso to restore 165 

three-dimensional structured illumination microscopy (3D-SIM) 9,39 datasets of fixed U2OS cells 166 

labeled with Alexa Fluor 488–immunolabeled microtubules. The restoration’s improved axial 167 

resolution better revealed the microtubule filament network in lateral and orthogonal views ( Fig. 168 

3a,b ). Compared to reconstructions obtained with CARE, CycleGAN, SelfNet, and SSAI3D, 169 

DeepIso substantially improved the filament bundle continuity in axial views, closely resembling 170 

the ground truth reference provided by the hardware-based axial resolution enhancement approach, 171 

four-beam SIM (4B-SIM) 9 . Inspection of selected regions ( Fig. 3c ) confirmed that DeepIso 172 

effectively recovered submicron axial features and suppressed stripe-like or granular artifacts that 173 

persisted in other learning-based methods or hardware-based methods ( Supplementary Fig. 3 ). 174 

Quantitative evaluation using normalized cross-correlation (NCC) in the axial direction ( Fig. 3d )175 

also demonstrated that DeepIso achieved the highest structural consistency with the ground truth. 176 

The corresponding normalized axial intensity profiles ( Fig. 3e ) also showed that DeepIso closely 177 

matched the 4B-SIM profile, indicating faithful recovery of axial modulation depth and resolution. 178 

Next, we tested DeepIso on mitochondria in live U2OS cells transfected with mEmerald– 179 

Tomm20, acquired by dual-view light sheet microscopy 12,13,22 , which offers near-isotropic 180 

resolution by jointly deconvolving the raw data from two orthogonal views ( Fig. 3f ). We evaluated 181 

the performance of DeepIso on single-view stacks acquired at 0.8 detection NA (SPIM), against 182 7

which we also had ground truth dual-view reconstructions (diSPIM, ground truth). Even under this 183 

relatively modest NA, DeepIso produced isotropic restorations with better mitochondrial 184 

membrane continuity and network morphology compared to the input data. In representative axial 185 

slices ( Fig. 3g ), DeepIso outperformed CycleGAN, SelfNet, and SSAI3D, recovering fine axial 186 

mitochondrial details that remained blurred, fragmented, or artifact-prone in other methods, and 187 

more closely approached the dual-view reconstruction reference. Fourier-domain analysis ( Fig. 3h )188 

further confirmed the greater axial-frequency support resembling diSPIM, consistent with 189 

improved axial resolution and contrast. Quantitatively, DeepIso achieved the highest PSNR and 190 

SSIM and the lowest RMSE across all tested methods ( Fig. 3i ), indicating superior restoration 191 

fidelity and structural isotropy relative to the diSPIM reference. Together, these results demonstrate 192 

that DeepIso robustly enhances axial resolution in both 3D-SIM of immunolabeled U2OS 193 

microtubules and single-view SPIM data of live U2OS mitochondria, narrowing the gap between 194 

anisotropic raw inputs and isotropic ground-truth references. 195

2.4. DeepIso achieves isotropic resolution across multi-modality imaging 197 

We next examined whether DeepIso could generalize to conventional diffraction-limited imaging 198 

modalities, focusing on confocal fluorescence microscopy, which typically suffers from 199 

pronounced axial–lateral resolution anisotropy. We evaluated DeepIso on a volumetric Thy1-eYFP 200 

mouse neuron dataset 33 acquired by confocal fluorescence microscopy with optical sectioning 33 .201 

As reported for this dataset, optical sectioning produces a stark contrast between lateral and axial 202 

resolution, with highly anisotropic voxel sampling (0.207×0.207×1 μm³) in the raw volume. 203 

DeepIso resampled the volume and produced near isotropic resolution with isotropic voxels 204 

(0.207×0.207×0.207 μm³) ( Fig. 4a ). The maximum-intensity projections of axial views ( Fig. 4b,d )205 

revealed markedly improved axial continuity and contrast compared with the original confocal 206 

inputs. Magnified subregions and corresponding Fourier spectra ( Fig. 4c,e,f ) further indicate 207 

enhanced recovery of axial high-frequency components and expanded axial-frequency support in 208 

the Fourier domain, enabling finer delineation of densely interwoven neuronal processes with 209 

consistent morphology across viewing directions. Line profiles extracted along the axial direction 210 

(Fig. 4g,h ) showed sharper peak separation and reduced axial broadening after DeepIso restoration, 211 

while local region analysis ( Fig. 4i ) corroborated isotropic frequency recovery across the dataset. 212 

We further evaluated DeepIso on experimental super-resolution 3D-SIM datasets 9 . In 213 

bacterial membrane imaging ( Fig. 5a–d ), DeepIso markedly improved axial contrast and reduced 214 8

axial elongation, enabling clearer delineation of the two closely spaced membrane layers within 215 

individual bacteria, which appear axially smeared in the native 3D-SIM volume. Consistent with 216 

the visual restoration, Fourier analysis ( Fig. 5b ) showed an expanded axial-frequency support after 217 

DeepIso, and the corresponding magnified view comparisons and line profiles ( Fig. 5c,d )218 

confirmed enhanced peak separation and reduced axial broadening. 219 

We next applied DeepIso to 3D-SIM images of U2OS outer mitochondrial membranes 9

220 

(Fig. 5e–j ). DeepIso restored a continuous and morphologically coherent mitochondrial network 221 

in lateral views ( Fig. 5e ) and substantially sharpened mitochondrial boundaries along the axial 222 

direction ( Fig. 5f,i ). Line profiles ( Fig. 5g ) revealed increased axial modulation depth and 223 

improved separation of adjacent structures, while enlarged ROIs ( Fig. 5h,j ) highlighted recovery 224 

of fine axial details with reduced striping artifacts. Fourier spectra from selected regions ( Fig. 5j )225 

further supported the restoration of higher axial-frequency components compared with 226 

conventional 3D-SIM. 227 

Together, these results indicate that DeepIso can enhance axial resolution and 3D structural 228 

fidelity across both diffraction-limited and super-resolution modalities without requiring explicit 229 

PSF specification or manual modality-specific parameter tuning, while using the same training and 230 

adapting protocol per modality. 231

2.5. DeepIso facilitates reliable segmentation and cell tracking for time-lapse data 233 

To further demonstrate the applicability of DeepIso to long-term volumetric imaging, we applied 234 

it to dual-view light sheet microscopy datasets of zebrafish embryos expressing Lyn–eGFP under 235 

the claudin B (cldnb) promoter 22 , which labels epithelial cell membranes around the lateral-line 236 

primordium ( Fig. 6a ). The single view raw data (SPIM) exhibited characteristic axial blurring and 237 

discontinuities in membrane junctions, particularly along the apical–basal direction. DeepIso 238 

restoration significantly improved axial continuity and junctional delineation, producing 239 

volumetric restoration comparable to the two-view reconstruction results (diSPIM), but without 240 

requiring explicit two-view data acquisition and fusion ( Fig. 6b ). 241 

Fourier-domain analysis ( Fig. 6c ) revealed that DeepIso expanded the recoverable axial-242 

frequency range relative to the single view raw (SPIM) and corresponding deconvolution (Deconv) 243 

images, reflecting enhanced resolution along the optical axis. Enlarged ROIs (Fig. 6d,e )244 

demonstrated smooth and isotropic recovery of epithelial boundaries with enhanced contrast, 245 

allowing clear visualization of individual cell membranes within the densely packed epithelial 246 9

layers. Segmentation of the DeepIso restored volumes ( Fig. 6f,g ) further confirmed accurate and 247 

continuous delineation of cell boundaries across the primordium, in contrast to the fragmented or 248 

partially fused junctions in the original single view data. 249 

Importantly, DeepIso facilitated manual tracking and automatic segmentation in long-term 250 

time-lapse imaging. In the 6.25-hour light sheet microscopy recording, DeepIso restored isotropic 251 

morphology and continuity of the migrating primordium ( Fig. 6h ) while preserving dynamic 252 

subcellular features. Two distinct regions of interest ( Fig. 6i,j ) revealed two immune cells (arrows) 253 

migrating between surrounding skin cells: one cell in the period of 0–165 minutes ( Fig. 6i ) and 254 

another in the period of 315–375 minutes ( Fig. 6j ), highlighting DeepIso’s ability to maintain clear 255 

cell outlines and continuous elongated structures across frames. 256 

Together, these results demonstrate that DeepIso not only restores isotropic spatial 257 

resolution in light-sheet microscopy but also enables long-term volumetric imaging with improved 258 

spatiotemporal resolution. By recovering high-frequency axial information directly from single-259 

view raw data, DeepIso eliminates the dependence on multi-view data acquisition and complex 260 

multiview fusion procedures, thus providing a scalable framework for high-fidelity live-cell and 261 

developmental imaging. 262

3. Discussion and conclusion 264 

In this study, we introduced DeepIso, a self-supervised framework for restoring isotropic resolution 265 

in three-dimensional microscopy. By leveraging a two-stage training paradigm that integrates 266 

synthetic pretraining with adaptive inference, DeepIso learns modality-independent isotropic 267 

features directly from the data, eliminating the need for paired high-resolution supervision or 268 

explicit multi-view fusion. Across diverse imaging modalities, including 3D-SIM, SPIM, diSPIM, 269 

and confocal microscopy, DeepIso consistently enhanced axial resolution, improved structural 270 

isotropy, and preserved fine morphological details, while minimizing artifacts and noise 271 

amplification. 272 

Unlike previous learning-based restoration approaches that rely on explicit supervision (e.g., 273 

CARE) or adversarial domain translation (e.g., CycleGAN, SelfNet), DeepIso performs internal 274 

adaptation through latent-space consistency. The encoder–decoder pair (IsoEnc/IsoDec) remains 275 

fixed during adaptation, ensuring stable feature representation and preventing overfitting to dataset-276 

specific noise patterns. This design enables robust generalization across different imaging systems, 277 10 

as evidenced by DeepIso’s strong performance on both simulated benchmarks and experimental 278 

datasets, including live-cell and organismal imaging. 279 

A key advantage of DeepIso lies in its ability to recover high-frequency axial components 280 

from single-view anisotropic inputs, without the need for external calibration data. Fourier-domain 281 

analysis confirmed that DeepIso effectively expands the axial-frequency support, thereby restoring 282 

isotropic point-spread responses and volumetric contrast. This improvement in isotropy directly 283 

benefits downstream analyses, such as segmentation and morphological quantification. Beyond 284 

static imaging, DeepIso demonstrated substantial utility in time-lapse volumetric analysis. In 285 

diSPIM recordings of developing zebrafish embryos, the method restored continuous epithelial 286 

boundaries and facilitates manual tracking of immune-cell migration over extended durations with 287 

high spatiotemporal fidelity ( Fig. 6 ). Such capability underscores DeepIso’s potential for studying 288 

dynamic biological processes that require both gentle illumination and precise 3D reconstruction, 289 

such as tissue morphogenesis, organ development, and intracellular transport. This application 290 

highlights DeepIso’s robustness in both high-resolution anatomical imaging and time-resolved 291 

dynamic analysis. 292 

Although DeepIso demonstrates strong isotropic restoration performance under matched 293 

imaging conditions, several limitations remain. First, its generalization across heterogeneous 294 

biological samples is inherently constrained. In practice, different specimen types exhibit 295 

substantially different structural organizations, signal-to-noise characteristics, and anisotropic 296 

degradation patterns. Consequently, optimal performance typically requires dataset-specific 297 

training or adaptation, rather than reliance on a single universal model. This limitation reflects a 298 

broader challenge in microscopy image restoration, where strong domain shifts arise from 299 

biological diversity and optical variability. Future work may explore generalized pretrained 300 

representations 40,41 or meta-learning strategies 42 to improve cross-sample generalization with 301 

reduced retraining cost. Second, DeepIso introduces additional computational cost associated with 302 

its multi-component training framework. For example, in our experiments on confocal Mouse Thy1 303 

neuron data, training of the IsoNet module required approximately 4.8 hours, while training of the 304 

IsoEnc/IsoDec module required approximately 6.0 hours on a single NVIDIA RTX 4080 SUPER 305 

GPU. The adapting-inference stage converged substantially faster, requiring approximately 13 306 

minutes for 12,000 iterations. Notably, the training of IsoNet and IsoEnc/IsoDec is structurally 307 

independent. When computational resources permit, these two components can be trained 308 

concurrently on separate GPUs, which can effectively reduce the overall wall-clock training time. 309 11 

Once training and adaptation are complete, DeepIso can be directly applied to raw biological image 310 

volumes in a purely feed-forward manner, analogous to conventional end-to-end restoration 311 

networks such as CARE 23 or RCAN3D 26 . In this inference stage, no additional optimization or 312 

parameter updating is required, and large volumetric datasets can be processed efficiently using 313 

standard block-wise reconstruction strategies. Third, DeepIso conceptually could be extended to 314 

laterally anisotropic data, but this would require redesigning the network to recover isotropy from 315 

only one high-resolution lateral direction. We expect substantial additional development and 316 

validation, so we leave this as future work and only note it here. Finally, future extensions of 317 

DeepIso may benefit from integration with multi-view fusion strategies or physics-informed priors, 318 

particularly for complex specimens exhibiting strong light scattering, refractive index 319 

heterogeneity, or severe optical aberrations. Such hybrid designs may further enhance 320 

reconstruction robustness and extend applicability to challenging biological imaging scenarios. 321 

Overall, DeepIso provides a unified and scalable framework for isotropic 3D reconstruction 322 

across a broad range of fluorescence microscopy modalities. Its self-supervised learning scheme, 323 

internal adaptation, and strong cross-domain generalization make it a powerful tool for next-324 

generation volumetric imaging, which can bridge the gap between diffraction-limited acquisitions 325 

and isotropic, artifact-free reconstructions suitable for quantitative biological analysis. DeepIso 326 

thus offers a significant step forward in enabling high-quality, high-fidelity imaging in both static 327 

and dynamic biological studies. 328

Methods 330 

The DeepIso framework adopts a modular architecture composed of an isotropic restoration 331 

backbone (IsoNet) and a latent feature encoder–decoder module (IsoEnc/IsoDec). The model is 332 

first pretrained on synthetically generated anisotropic–isotropic patch pairs to establish a stable 333 

isotropy-aware mapping. During adapting inference, the latent feature learned by IsoEnc is 334 

combined with experimental observations through IsoDec, enabling unsupervised refinement 335 

under real imaging conditions. This two-stage strategy ensures both reconstruction fidelity and 336 

robustness to system-dependent anisotropy. 337 

Network architecture 338 

IsoNet was implemented as a compact residual channel attention network (RCAN) backbone 26 . A 339 

single channel input was linearly standardized from 0 to 1 into minus 1 to 1, passed through a 3 by 340 

3 feature extraction layer with 32 channels, and processed by five residual groups, each containing 341 12 

five residual channel attention blocks with reduction ratio 8. Each block used two 3 by 3 342 

convolutions with a ReLU after the first, followed by channel attention based on global average 343 

pooling and 1 ×1 bottleneck layers. A long skip connection bridged the residual group stack, and a 344 

final 3 ×3 convolution produced a single channel output that was mapped back to 0 to 1. 345 

IsoEnc and IsoDec were implemented jointly as a two-branch RCAN based architecture. Both 346 

inputs were first linearly standardized from 0 to 1 into minus 1 to 1. IsoEnc encoded the first input 347 

using an RCAN style encoder with 64 feature channels and three residual groups, and produced a 348 

1024 dimensional latent representation via global average pooling followed by a 1 by 1 convolution. 349 

IsoDec conditioned the reconstruction on both the latent code and the second input. The latent code 350 

was expanded by a 1 by 1 convolution and bilinear upsampling to match the spatial patch size, then 351 

concatenated with features extracted from the second input by a symmetric RCAN stem. The fused 352 

features were refined by three residual groups and projected to output, which was mapped back to 353 

0 to 1 by the inverse linear transform. 354

Inference and block-wise reconstruction strategy 448 

To accommodate large volumetric datasets exceeding GPU memory limits, DeepIso inference was 449 

performed in a block-wise manner with controlled spatial overlap between adjacent tiles. Each 3D 450 

input volume was first divided into overlapping sub-volumes (typically 64³–128³ voxels per block) 451 

with an overlap margin of 16–32 voxels along each axis. The overlapping regions were smoothly 452 

blended using a linear weighting window to avoid boundary discontinuities after reconstruction. 453 16 

Before inference, anisotropic raw data were aligned through the same pixel-alignment procedure 454 

used during training, i.e., one spatial dimension was downsampled to match the axial pixel size and 455 

subsequently upsampled to the lateral sampling interval. The aligned volumes were then processed 456 

by the pretrained IsoNet adapted through Adapting Inference Stage. 457 

All convolutional layers operated on isotropic receptive fields, ensuring that the network preserved 458 

structural consistency across the axial and lateral dimensions. The encoder and decoder 459 

components (IsoEnc/IsoDec) remained frozen during inference, maintaining a stable latent-space 460 

mapping learned during pretraining. The intermediate IsoNet weights, fine-tuned during the 461 

adaptive stage, handled residual anisotropy and dataset-specific characteristics such as refractive 462 

mismatch, optical aberration, and SNR variation. 463 

The restored sub-volumes were finally stitched into the full 3D isotropic reconstruction using 464 

weighted averaging in the overlap regions, producing seamless volumes suitable for quantitative 465 

downstream analyses such as segmentation, tracking, and Fourier-domain isotropy evaluation. 466 

Dataset preparation for pretraining 467 

In Stage-1: Pretraining, a paired pretraining dataset was constructed by extracting 2D patches from 468 

3D TIFF volumes and generating two corresponding representations for each patch: (i) a laterally 469 

well-resolved reference patch, which serves as a proxy for isotropic lateral resolution, and (ii) an 470 

anisotropic patch, synthetically degraded to emulate direction-dependent resolution loss. In 471 

practice, the reference patches were sampled from lateral view and treated as the high-quality target, 472 

whereas the anisotropic counterparts were produced through controlled blurring and optional 473 

resampling so that the paired data explicitly encode an isotropic-to-anisotropic mapping. 474 

All volumes were loaded from the input directory and, unless otherwise stated, intensity-475 

normalized by percentile-based 23 linear rescaling to map values into [0, 1], with optional clipping 476 

to suppress outliers. Patch extraction was performed slice-wise along the axial dimension. For each 477 

𝑧𝑧 -slice, a fixed number of square crops (default 64; 10 patches per slice) were drawn at random. 478 

Because raw microscopy volumes frequently contain extensive low-signal background, patch 479 

selection was constrained by a rejection criterion to enrich informative structures: candidate crops 480 

were repeatedly proposed (up to 1000 attempts) and retained only when their mean intensity 481 

exceeded 70% of the corresponding volume-wide mean. 482 

The anisotropic counterparts were generated from the same reference crops using an orientation-483 

dependent blur model, followed by an optional downsample–upsample step that mimics under-484 

sampling. Gaussian blur kernel collection was assembled in two stages. First, a set of base Gaussian 485 17 

kernels with fixed support (size 31 pixels) was created by varying the standard deviation (sampled 486 

from 3 to 101 in steps of 2, retaining the first K kernels where K= kernel_number ), and each kernel 487 

was normalized to ensure consistent scaling. Second, to reproduce directionally biased degradation, 488 

each base kernel was rotated in-plane to multiple orientations, with angles uniformly distributed 489 

over [−90 ∘, 90 ∘]according to angle_number bins; the complete kernel library was saved to disk to 490 

facilitate reproducibility. For each reference patch, the corresponding anisotropic patch was 491 

synthesized by FFT-based convolution with a selected kernel while preserving the patch size via 492 

padding. When enabled, a pixel size alignment procedure was applied by first resizing the 493 

convolved patch to a lower resolution determined by downsampling_rate and then interpolating it 494 

back to the original patch size, thereby introducing scale-dependent smoothing consistent with 495 

anisotropic sampling. 496 

A deterministic patch-level split was used to obtain stable training, validation, and test partitions. 497 

Within each group of ten sampled patches, one patch was assigned to the validation set and one to 498 

the test set, while the remaining eight were used for training (approximately 8:1:1). All paired 499 

patches were saved as TIFF files into the corresponding train/val/test subdirectories. 500 

Dataset preparation for adapting inference 501 

In the State-2: Adapting Inference, an unaligned patch dataset was derived directly from 502 

experimental 3D volumes so that the model could be internally adapted to real acquisition statistics 503 

without presuming cross-view correspondence. Each TIFF volume was loaded from disk and, 504 

unless stated otherwise, intensity-normalized by percentile-based linear rescaling. Values were 505 

mapped to the range from 0 to 1, and clipping was applied to suppress out-of-range fluctuations. 506 

This normalization was applied consistently across volumes to reduce scale variability while 507 

retaining specimen-specific contrast relationships. 508 

Patch extraction was performed from orthogonal section families, since anisotropic sampling 509 

induces plane-dependent image statistics, particularly for sections that intersect the axial direction. 510 

Two sets of 2D crops were therefore generated. First, xz sections were formed by fixing the y index. 511 

Within each section, square patches of fixed size, by default 64 by 64 pixels, were randomly 512 

cropped in the xz plane, with multiple realizations drawn per section, by default 10 patches. In 513 

parallel, xy sections were formed by fixing the z index, followed by random cropping in the xy 514 

plane using the same patch size and sampling multiplicity. Because crops from the two section 515 

families were sampled independently with different random spatial coordinates, the resulting 516 

dataset was intentionally unpaired and spatially unaligned across views, which matches the 517 18 

adapting-inference setting in which only raw observations are available and explicit registration 518 

between orthogonal planes is not assumed. 519 

To ensure stable data split across runs, patches were assigned to training, validation, and test sets 520 

using a deterministic indexing rule. Within each sampling loop, one out of every ten patches was 521 

routed to validation and one to test, whereas the remaining patches were used for adaptation and 522 

training, yielding an approximate 8 to 1 to 1 partition. All patches were saved as individual TIFF 523 

files into the corresponding train, val, and test directories. When available, CuPy was used to 524 

accelerate array operations; otherwise, the same protocol was executed with NumPy on CPU, 525 

without changing the sampling procedure or the resulting dataset composition. 526

Fig. 1 | The two-stage DeepIso framework for self‑supervised isotropic resolution 616 

restoration. 617 

DeepIso transfers knowledge from diverse, synthetically generated degradation patterns to the 618 

anisotropic restoration of specific dataset through a pretraining‑then‑adaptation workflow . a,619 

Stage 1 (Pretraining): Supervised pretraining on synthetic data. An IsoNet backbone is trained to 620 

learn isotropic representations from artificially degraded data. Isotropic lateral slices are 621 

synthetically degraded using a collection of coarse axial blur kernels and pixel size alignment to 622 

generate diverse and realistic axial anisotropic candidates (Anisotropic Data). Then these axial 623 

anisotropic slices are fed into the IsoNet to learn the anisotropic-to-isotropic mapping by 624 

supervised pretraining. Meanwhile, an encoder module (IsoEnc) is concatenated to capture 625 

anisotropic structural features from anisotropic slices in the latent space, while a decoder module 626 

(IsoDec) is trained to utilize latent features to degrade the isotropic lateral slices by enforcing 627 

reconstruction consistency. IsoEnc and IsoDec are jointly updated to realize accurate latent 628 

feature representation. b, Stage 2 (Adapting Inference): The raw axial data (Axial Data) is first 629 

axially interpolated to match the axial pixel size to the lateral pixel size (Pixel Alignment) and get 630 

the aligned axial data for network input (Aligned Axial Data). These data are fed into IsoNet 631 

using transfer learning to get initial estimates (Restored Axial Data). Then these initial estimates 632 

together with the aligned axial data are used to generate the intermediate anisotropic outputs 633 

(Latent Anisotropic Output) using locked IsoEnc/IsoDec pair. By optimizing the consistency 634 

between the real axial data (Aligned Axial Data) and intermediate outputs (Latent Anisotropic 635 

Output), the IsoNet is iteratively updated and produces the final restored data (Restored Axial 636 

Data). 637
